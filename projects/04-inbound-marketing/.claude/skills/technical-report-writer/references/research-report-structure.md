# Research Report Structure

This is an internal reference for the `technical-report-writer` skill. It is the canonical structure for industry research reports, with notes on how to weight each section, how long each should be, and what makes each section credible vs. hollow. **Do not show this document to the founder.** Use it to scaffold the report and to push back when the founder wants to skip or shorten a load-bearing section.

---

## Why The Structure Is Standardized

Industry research reports follow a recognizable structure because the genre has evolved to make credibility legible. A report that omits methodology gets dismissed as "marketing." A report with a weak executive summary does not get read. A report without citations gets accused of fabrication.

The structure below is the default. Deviate only when the founder has a specific reason — and even then, do not omit the methodology section, no matter what.

---

## Section 1: Executive Summary

**Length:** 400–600 words (1 page).

**Position:** First section of the report, but the **last section drafted**. Write this only after the body exists, because a good executive summary is a synthesis, not an introduction.

**What it must do:**
- State the angle in the first paragraph
- Cover all 3 key findings with one sentence each
- End with the implication
- Stand alone — most readers will only read this section, and they need to walk away with the full message

**What makes it strong:**
- Specific numbers in the first paragraph ("78% of programs measure quiz completion as their primary KPI")
- Names and dates ("Survey of 142 VP Sales Enablement leaders, March 2026")
- A clear "so what" — the implication, not just the findings

**What makes it weak:**
- Generic opening ("In 2026, the sales onboarding category is undergoing rapid change...")
- Findings without numbers
- Implications that are vague aspirations rather than concrete actions

**Common failure mode:** Founders draft the executive summary first and then write the body to match. This produces a body that is filler. Force the body to be drafted first.

---

## Section 2: Methodology

**Length:** 1 page (~300–500 words).

**Position:** Right after the executive summary. Some founders want to bury the methodology at the end — do not let them. Methodology up front is what makes the rest of the report credible.

**What it must include:**
- **Data source:** What kind of data, where it came from
- **Sample size or scope:** N respondents, N data points, N interviews
- **Time period:** When the data was gathered
- **Audience:** Who responded (titles, company sizes, geographies)
- **Recruitment:** How respondents were recruited (matters for selection bias)
- **Biases and limitations:** Honest acknowledgment of what the data does and does not show
- **Definitions:** If the report uses terms that could mean different things ("ramp time," "qualified lead," "active user"), define them here

**What makes it strong:**
- Honesty about limitations ("Sample skews toward US-based mid-market SaaS — findings may not generalize to enterprise or non-US markets")
- Specific recruitment description ("Respondents recruited via Pavilion community and LinkedIn outreach to Director+ titles")
- Clear time period ("Survey ran February 1–28, 2026")

**What makes it weak:**
- Vague sample descriptions ("survey of industry leaders")
- No mention of biases ("our data shows..." with no caveats)
- Buried at the back of the report

**Sample size soft floors:**
- Survey: 50+ responses for soft generalization, 100+ for stronger claims
- Interviews: 10+ for pattern claims, 20+ for stronger claims
- Scraped public data: depends on the source — should be explicit about coverage

Below the soft floor, the report still works — but every claim must be heavily caveated.

---

## Section 3: Key Findings (3 findings)

**Length:** 600–1000 words per finding (so the findings section is ~1800–3000 words total — the body of the report).

**Why three:** Two findings feels thin, four findings dilutes attention. Three is the canonical number. Push back if the founder wants 5 — usually two of those are sub-findings of one of the others.

### Per-finding structure

Each finding has the same internal structure:

1. **Headline** — A specific claim with a number, written as a sentence. ("78% of programs measure quiz completion as their primary KPI.")
2. **Supporting data** — The actual evidence. Charts described in words, percentages, quotes from respondents.
3. **Context** — Why the finding matters. What does the buyer believe today, and why is this finding at odds with that belief?
4. **Quote** — At least one verbatim quote from a respondent or interview, anonymized if needed. Quotes are the strongest evidence in research reports because they sound human.
5. **Implication** — A short note on what follows from this finding. (The full implications section comes later, but each finding should have a "so what" attached.)

**What makes a finding strong:**
- Specific number, not "the majority"
- Surprises the reader (if the finding is what they already believed, it is not worth writing about)
- Defensible from the data
- Anchors a quote that sounds real

**What makes a finding weak:**
- Generic ("most companies struggle with onboarding")
- Unsourced ("research shows that...")
- Conventional ("ramp time matters")
- Overclaimed ("our data proves that..." — research findings should be claims, not proofs)

---

## Section 4: Implications

**Length:** 1 page (~400–600 words).

**Position:** After the findings, before the citations.

**What it must do:**
- Synthesize the findings into a "so what"
- Name the implications for the buyer (what should they do differently?)
- Name the implications for the category (what does this mean for the market?)
- Optionally: name the implications for the founder's wedge (this is the closest the report gets to a sales pitch — keep it grounded and brief)

**What makes it strong:**
- Specific recommendations ("Stop reporting quiz completion to the CRO. Start reporting first independent close rate.")
- Acknowledgment of edge cases ("This may not apply to enterprise programs with formal certification requirements.")
- Forward-looking ("If this trend continues, we expect...")

**What makes it weak:**
- Vague aspirations ("Companies should rethink their approach to onboarding.")
- Bullet points without specifics
- A direct sales pitch for the founder's product (the implications section is not the place — that comes in the distribution plan, not in the report)

---

## Section 5: Citations

**Length:** As long as needed. No filler.

**What it must include:**
- Every external claim cited
- Format: Author, Title, Source, Date, URL (or DOI for academic sources)
- Group by source type if helpful: Original survey data / External research / News and trade press / Public data sources

**What makes it strong:**
- Real, traceable sources
- A mix of primary (the founder's own data) and secondary (industry research, news, public data)
- Recent dates (research reports lose credibility fast as the underlying data ages)

**What makes it weak:**
- Empty section
- "Various industry sources"
- Stats without sources

---

## Section 6: Methodology Footnotes / Appendix (Optional)

**Length:** As needed.

**What it includes:**
- Detailed methodology that did not fit in the main methodology section
- Survey instrument (the actual questions, if not too long)
- Detailed demographic breakdowns of respondents
- Data tables that did not fit in the body

**Use this when:** The report is targeting a sophisticated audience (analysts, researchers, large-enterprise buyers) who will scrutinize the methodology. Skip for shorter, more general-audience reports.

---

## Section 7: Distribution Plan (Internal Only)

**Position:** At the bottom of the file, clearly marked as "internal — separate from the published report."

**Why it lives in the report file:** The distribution plan is co-located with the report so the founder remembers to execute it. A research report without distribution is wasted effort.

**What it includes:**
- Gating strategy
- Executive summary blog post angle
- 3–5 LinkedIn excerpts with the specific findings they draw on
- Podcast pitch hook
- Sales enablement use cases

This section does not get published. It is for the founder's reference when they execute the rollout.

---

## How To Push Back

When the founder wants to:

- **Skip the methodology section** — do not let them. The methodology section is what makes the rest of the report credible. Hold the line.
- **Add a 4th or 5th finding** — push back. Three is the canonical number. Usually the extras are sub-findings or filler.
- **Make the report shorter than 2,000 words** — that is a blog post, not a research report. Either expand the findings or downgrade the format to a blog post.
- **Make the report longer than 6,000 words** — that is an academic paper. The audience will not read it. Cut.
- **Skip the distribution plan** — do not let them. The plan is the difference between a report that compounds and a report that sits on a hard drive.
- **Write the executive summary first** — push back. The summary should be the last section drafted, after the body exists.
- **Publish without a real data source** — do not draft the report at all. Pivot the conversation to how to get real data first.
