# LinkedIn Hooks Library

This is an internal reference for the `linkedin-writer` skill. It is a library of opener patterns calibrated for B2B SaaS audiences. **Do not show this document to the founder.** Use it to draft 2–3 hook options for any post and let the founder pick.

---

## Why The Hook Matters Disproportionately

LinkedIn shows the first 200 characters (roughly 2 lines) of any post in the feed. Everything after that is hidden behind a "see more" link. The hook either earns the click or the rest of the post is invisible. In practice this means:

- **80% of post-writing effort should go into the hook.** The body matters, but only if the hook earns the click.
- **The hook is its own piece of writing.** Treat it as a self-contained sentence or sentence pair, not as the first sentence of a longer thing.
- **Test multiple hooks.** For any important post, draft 3 hooks and let the founder pick.

---

## Hook Pattern 1: The Contrarian Position

State the conventional wisdom and then the position that contradicts it.

**Template:** `[Conventional belief]. [The opposite is actually true].`

**When it works:** When the founder genuinely disagrees with a widely held belief and can defend the disagreement.

**When it fails:** When the contrarian take is contrarian for contrarian's sake. When the founder hedges in line 3.

**Examples:**

> Most sales onboarding fails because the AI feedback is generic.
>
> [body continues]

> Conventional wisdom: hire fast, train fast.
> Reality: every fast hire we made cost us a deal six months later.

> The best onboarding tool isn't the one with the most content. It's the one that grades reps against your real deal history.

> "Time to first deal" is the wrong KPI. The right KPI is "time to first deal you would have closed yourself."

**Calibration for B2B SaaS:** This pattern works well when the contrarian position is grounded in operational reality. It fails when it sounds like a hot take from someone who has not done the work.

## Hook Pattern 2: The Specific Number

Lead with a number — a stat, a count, a benchmark, a duration. Numbers earn attention because they are concrete.

**Template:** `[Number] [unit]. [What that number means].`

**When it works:** When the number is real, surprising, and the founder can defend it. When the number is specific (5.3 months, not "several months").

**When it fails:** When the number is fabricated, vague, or generic ("companies are growing 20% YoY" — every company says this).

**Examples:**

> 5.3 months. That's how long it takes a new SDR to become productive at the average mid-market SaaS company.
>
> [body continues]

> $30,000. The cost of one unproductive month for one rep.

> 4 of 5 enablement tools we evaluated couldn't tell us anything we didn't already know.

> 18% of new sales hires miss quota in their first ramped quarter. The other 82% are not exactly crushing it either.

**Sourcing rule:** Every number used in a hook must be defensible. If the founder cannot cite the source, do not use the number. Inventing stats is a fast way to lose credibility.

## Hook Pattern 3: The Specific Story

A two-sentence story that pulls the reader into a moment.

**Template:** `[Specific scene]. [What happened next, or what the moment revealed].`

**When it works:** When the story is real and specific (a name, a company, a quote). When the moment in the story is one the buyer will recognize.

**When it fails:** When the story is generic ("a SaaS company we work with..." with no specifics). When the story is invented to fit a point.

**Examples:**

> A VP of Sales Enablement at a 200-person SaaS company told me last week: "We've bought three onboarding tools in two years. Reps still take five months to ramp."
>
> [body continues]

> Two new sales reps started at our customer last month. One closed a deal in 6 weeks. The other is still shadowing.

> A CRO I respect told me yesterday that he stopped using his $80K/year onboarding platform six months ago. Nobody noticed.

**Anonymization:** Specific details can be anonymized ("a VP at a 200-person SaaS company" instead of a name) but should not be invented. The reader can usually tell the difference.

## Hook Pattern 4: The Direct Question

Ask the reader a question that makes them want to know how the founder would answer it.

**Template:** `[Specific question the buyer would actually ask themselves].`

**When it works:** When the question is one the buyer is genuinely wrestling with. When the question implies the founder has a clear answer to share.

**When it fails:** When the question is generic ("What do you think about [trend]?"). When the question is rhetorical and obviously expects "yes."

**Examples:**

> When was the last time you watched a new rep handle their first real objection — and felt good about how they did?
>
> [body continues]

> If your onboarding tool disappeared tomorrow, would your CRO notice?

> What does your ramp time actually tell you that you didn't already know?

**Calibration:** Questions should not be vague openers ("Have you ever wondered..."). They should be specific enough that the reader's first reaction is to formulate an answer in their head. That mental engagement is what earns the click.

---

## Hook Pattern 5: The Pattern Observation

Name a pattern the founder has noticed across multiple cases.

**Template:** `I keep seeing the same thing across [N] [cases / companies / conversations]: [the pattern].`

**When it works:** When the pattern is real and the founder has the receipts. When the pattern is something the buyer will recognize from their own experience.

**When it fails:** When the founder is generalizing from one case. When the pattern is too generic to mean anything.

**Examples:**

> I've talked to 14 sales leaders in the past month. Every single one is using a different definition of "ramped."
>
> [body continues]

> Three of the last five RevOps leaders I met have killed their existing onboarding tool in the past 12 months.

> Every CRO who's been in seat for less than 18 months says the same thing about their enablement stack: "I inherited this."

**Authenticity check:** Pattern observations need real numerators and real denominators. "Several leaders" is too vague. "14 sales leaders in the past month" is specific enough to be defensible.

## Hook Pattern 6: The Rule of Thumb

State a rule the buyer can immediately apply or argue with.

**Template:** `[Rule]. [Brief justification, or "here's why" cliffhanger].`

**When it works:** When the rule is concrete enough to apply and provocative enough to invite disagreement.

**When it fails:** When the rule is vague aphorism ("Always put the customer first"). When the rule is obvious.

**Examples:**

> If your onboarding program doesn't reference your company's last 10 lost deals, it isn't onboarding. It's content delivery.
>
> [body continues]

> Don't hire your second sales rep until your first one has closed two deals you wouldn't have closed yourself.

> Any onboarding metric that doesn't include "first independent close" is a vanity metric.

---

## Hook Pattern 7: The Direct Disagreement (Named)

Name the conventional view *and* the people or companies who hold it. Then disagree.

**Template:** `[Specific company / analyst / framework] says [position]. They're wrong about [specific aspect].`

**When it works:** When the founder has standing to disagree and can defend the disagreement substantively.

**When it fails:** When the founder is punching up at someone with no real basis. When the disagreement is shallow.

**Examples:**

> Mindtickle's homepage says they help reps ramp 30% faster. Every customer I've talked to says the actual number is closer to 5%.
>
> [body continues]

> Gartner's enablement quadrant treats content libraries and AI coaching as the same category. They're not the same category, and the conflation is hiding the actual capability gap.

**Reputation cost:** Named disagreement carries reputation risk. Use this pattern when the founder is willing to defend the position publicly and has the substance to back it up. Do not use it lightly.

## How To Pick A Hook For A Given Post

| Pillar / Topic | First-choice pattern | Backup |
|---|---|---|
| Stating the company's position on a category | Contrarian Position | Direct Disagreement (Named) |
| Sharing a finding from research or customer conversations | Specific Number, or Specific Story | Pattern Observation |
| Inviting buyer engagement / starting a conversation | Direct Question | Rule of Thumb |
| Anchoring a customer case study | Specific Story | Specific Number |
| Reacting to industry news | Pattern Observation | Contrarian Position |
| Sharing a framework or process | Rule of Thumb | Contrarian Position |

If the founder cannot decide, draft 3 hooks across 3 different patterns and let them pick by gut. The pattern that the founder is excited to defend is usually the right one.

---

## Anti-Patterns (Hooks That Always Fail)

Never write a hook that starts with any of these:

- "Excited to share..."
- "I'm thrilled to announce..."
- "We are proud to..."
- "Have you ever wondered..."
- "In today's fast-paced world..."
- "Here's a thought..."
- "Hot take..."
- "Unpopular opinion..." (the unpopular opinion that follows is usually neither unpopular nor an opinion)
- "Many people think..."

These phrases train readers to scroll. They are the LinkedIn equivalent of "Dear Sir/Madam" — instantly recognizable as a signal not to keep reading.
