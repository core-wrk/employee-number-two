# Founder vs. Company Voice

This is an internal reference for the `founder-linkedin-builder` skill. It contains worked examples of the same topic written as company-voice (what `linkedin-writer` would produce) and as founder-voice (what this skill produces), with annotations explaining what changed and why. **Do not show this document to the founder.** Use it to calibrate the divergence and to teach the founder when a draft is collapsing back into the company voice.

---

## Why The Two Voices Should Sound Different

A founder is allowed to be:
- **Personal** — first person, with specific stories from their own work
- **Uncertain** — willing to admit live questions and unresolved bets
- **Opinionated** — sharper takes than the company can defend in a sales conversation
- **Specific** — names, numbers, moments, anonymized but real

A company is required to be:
- **Confident** — the company has to defend its claims to buyers
- **Consistent** — every employee should be able to talk about the company in the same voice
- **Buyer-focused** — the company speaks to the buyer's reality, not the founder's curiosity
- **Defensible** — every claim must hold up to scrutiny in a sales call

When the two voices collapse into each other, the founder sounds like a press release and the company sounds like a single person. Both lose credibility. The point of `founder-linkedin-builder` (and the reason it is a separate skill from `linkedin-writer`) is to keep the two distinct.

## Worked Example 1: Position on a Category Problem

**Topic:** Most sales onboarding tools fail because their AI feedback is generic, not tied to specific deal history.

### Company-voice version (what `linkedin-writer` produces)

> Most sales onboarding fails because the AI feedback is generic.
>
> Reps pass the quizzes but still don't know how to handle real objections from real buyers.
>
> The fix isn't more content. It's grading new reps against your actual deal history — the calls that closed, the calls that didn't, the patterns that repeat.
>
> Mindtickle, Lessonly, and Second Nature all have great features. None of them have your deal history.
>
> The first onboarding tool that grades against the company's own deals will own this category.
>
> Curious — does your team's onboarding actually reference the deals you've closed?

**Annotations:**
- Third person ("the fix is")
- Claim-forward ("will own this category")
- Buyer-focused CTA ("does your team's onboarding...")
- Defensible — every claim could be argued in a sales call
- 6 paragraphs, ~600 characters

### Founder-voice version (what this skill produces)

> I spent four years at a 200-person SaaS company watching us buy three different onboarding tools.
>
> All three were "AI-powered." All three made our reps better at quizzes. None of them made our reps better at the things that actually got deals across the line.
>
> I think the gap is structural. These tools never see the company's actual deal history — the calls that closed, the ones that died, the patterns the senior reps could spot in their sleep. They're trained on generic best practices.
>
> I'm not certain this is the only thing that matters. But I am pretty sure no onboarding tool that ignores deal history will ever feel like more than a content library with a chatbot bolted on.
>
> Curious if anyone reading this has seen a tool that does it differently. I might be wrong.

**Annotations:**
- First person ("I spent four years")
- Personal experience as standing ("at a 200-person SaaS company")
- Admits live question ("I'm not certain", "I might be wrong")
- Sharper than company voice ("a content library with a chatbot bolted on" — too sharp for the company to defend in a sales call, but defensible from a founder's mouth)
- Ends with a real question, not a rhetorical one
- 5 paragraphs, ~750 characters

### What Changed

| Dimension | Company | Founder |
|---|---|---|
| Person | Implicit third ("the fix is...") | First ("I spent four years...") |
| Standing | Brand authority | Personal experience |
| Certainty | High ("will own this category") | Mixed ("I'm pretty sure", "I might be wrong") |
| Sharpness | Restrained ("great features. None of them have your deal history.") | Sharp ("content library with a chatbot bolted on") |
| CTA | Generic question ("does your team's onboarding...") | Real question that invites disagreement ("might be wrong") |

## Worked Example 2: Story from Experience

**Topic:** Quizzes are a comfort metric in onboarding programs.

### Company-voice version

> Quiz completion is a comfort metric.
>
> It tells you the rep showed up and clicked through the modules. It does not tell you whether they can handle a real objection on a real call.
>
> The metric that matters is "first independent close" — the first deal a rep closed without manager intervention. That number predicts ramp time. Quiz completion does not.
>
> If your enablement dashboard only reports quiz completion, you're flying blind.

**Annotations:**
- Third person, prescriptive
- Direct, claim-forward
- Defensible in a sales call
- Slightly impersonal — sounds like a management book

### Founder-voice version

> A VP of Sales Enablement told me last month that her dashboard says her onboarding program is 92% successful.
>
> Then I asked her to define successful. It turned out 92% was the share of new reps who completed the quizzes.
>
> She wasn't lying. She wasn't even being lazy. The dashboard her enablement tool gave her measured what was easy to measure, and she'd been reporting that number to her CRO for two quarters.
>
> The actual question — how many of those reps closed an independent deal in their first 90 days — wasn't in the dashboard at all.
>
> Quiz completion is a comfort metric. It feels like data. It is not predictive of anything that matters.
>
> I keep meeting enablement leaders who are reporting comfort metrics to their CROs. I think this is one of the most quietly damaging patterns in B2B sales right now.

**Annotations:**
- First person, story-based
- Specific moment ("told me last month", "92%")
- Empathetic to the protagonist (she wasn't lying or lazy)
- Sharper conclusion than company voice ("quietly damaging")
- Anonymized but real
- 6 paragraphs, ~1000 characters

### What Changed

| Dimension | Company | Founder |
|---|---|---|
| Frame | Prescription | Story |
| Data | Generic ("the metric that matters") | Specific ("92% completion") |
| Empathy | Absent | Present (the VP isn't villainized) |
| Sharpness | Direct | Sharper conclusion at the end |
| Length | ~370 characters | ~1000 characters |
| Reader response invited | None | Recognition / agreement |

## Worked Example 3: Live Question Post (Founder-only Format)

**Topic:** Whether the founder's product should integrate with Gong first or Chorus first.

The company would not post about this — it is an internal product decision and surfacing it publicly might confuse buyers. But the founder posting about it is high-trust content: it shows the founder is thinking openly, invites the audience's input, and creates conversational entry points with both vendors' user bases.

### Founder-voice version

> A live question I'm wrestling with right now and would love input on.
>
> We're building an onboarding tool that grades new sales reps against their company's actual deal history. The data lives in two places for most of our target customers: the CRM (Salesforce / HubSpot) and the call intelligence tool (Gong / Chorus).
>
> Salesforce is non-negotiable — every customer has it. The call intelligence side is where I'm uncertain.
>
> Gong has the bigger market share and the better trajectory toward AI coaching. Integrating with Gong first probably gets us more customers. But it also puts us in a more competitive position the moment Gong's own AI coaching gets serious — and they're moving fast.
>
> Chorus has fewer customers but ZoomInfo's distribution and a less competitive product roadmap. Integrating with Chorus first is a smaller market but a clearer wedge.
>
> I lean Gong-first because of the customer count, but I'm not certain. If you're building or buying in this space, what would you do?

**Annotations:**
- Explicitly framed as a live question
- Honest about the tradeoff (does not pretend the answer is obvious)
- Specific names (Gong, Chorus, Salesforce, HubSpot)
- Ends with a real question, not a rhetorical one
- Founder admits leaning ("I lean Gong-first") but stays open
- Could not be a company post — the company would have to commit to an answer

**Why the company cannot post this:**
The company has to take a position with buyers. If the company says "we are still deciding," it sounds undecided and weakens trust. The founder saying "I am still deciding" reads as thoughtful and open. Same content, opposite reception.

## Common Failure Modes (When The Founder Voice Collapses Back)

### Failure 1: First person but corporate

> I'm excited to share that we're building something new in the sales onboarding space.

This is the company voice with "I" pasted in. Same problems: generic opener, no story, no standing, no specific detail. Rewrite.

### Failure 2: Personal but performative

> Hot take: most sales onboarding is broken.
>
> Here's why ↓
>
> 1. Quizzes don't predict anything
> 2. Content libraries go stale
> 3. AI feedback is generic
>
> The future of enablement isn't more content. It's deal-grounded coaching.
>
> Agree? Disagree? Drop a comment 👇

This is "founder-speak" — the LinkedIn-influencer rhetorical pattern that has nothing to do with the actual founder. Listicle structure, dramatic spacing, vague conclusion, performative CTA. Rewrite.

### Failure 3: Personal but punchless

> I've been thinking a lot about sales onboarding lately. There are some interesting trends emerging in this space, particularly around AI-driven coaching tools. I'm curious what others have observed.

This is too cautious to earn attention. The founder sounds like they are afraid to take a position. Push them to be more specific and more opinionated.

### Failure 4: Personal but unmoored from experience

> Most sales onboarding fails because the AI feedback is generic. I've seen this happen at countless companies.

"Countless companies" is the giveaway. The founder is speaking with the authority of experience but not grounding the claim in specific cases. Rewrite as "I saw this at three customers in the past month" or "I spent four years inside a 200-person SaaS company that bought three different tools."

## How To Coach The Founder Through Voice Calibration

When the founder pushes back on a draft, the question is usually one of three things:

1. **"This sounds too sharp."** — The founder is uncomfortable with the level of opinion. Decide together: is the sharpness defensible? If yes, hold the line. If no, soften — but do not soften to the point of being a company post.

2. **"This sounds too personal."** — The founder is uncomfortable being vulnerable. This is usually the moment where the post is actually working. Push back gently: "This is the move that distinguishes founder voice from company voice. Are you willing to be uncomfortable for a draft, then decide?"

3. **"This doesn't sound like me."** — The founder is right and you should rewrite. Reread `founder-profile.md` for cues on the founder's natural register and try again. The founder is the authority on their own voice.

The mistake to avoid: collapsing toward the company voice every time the founder pushes back. The whole point of the skill is to maintain a distinct voice. Hold the line on first-person, on specific stories, on real questions.
