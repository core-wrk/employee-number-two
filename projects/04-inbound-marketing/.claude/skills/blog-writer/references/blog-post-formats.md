# Blog Post Formats

This is an internal reference for the `blog-writer` skill. It describes the most common archetypes of B2B blog posts, when each works, when each fails, and the structural skeleton for each. **Do not show this document to the founder.** Use it to recommend a format that fits the post's intent and to structure the outline accordingly.

---

## How To Use

Once the founder has stated the post's intent (SEO / distribution / founder POV / launch) and the angle, look up the format that best fits. Each format has a structural skeleton — use it to scaffold the outline you walk the founder through. Do not lock the founder into a format if they have a clear reason to deviate.

---

## Format 1: The Opinionated POV

**Best for:** Founder POV intent. Posts that take a position and earn the reader's attention by being willing to be wrong.

**When it works:** When the founder has genuine experience or evidence behind the take. When the position contradicts conventional wisdom in the category. When the founder is willing to defend the take publicly.

**When it fails:** When the take is contrarian for contrarian's sake. When the founder is performing edginess rather than expressing a real belief. When the post hedges in the second paragraph.

**Structural skeleton:**

1. **Cold open with the position** (1 paragraph) — State the take in the first sentence. No setup, no "in this post we will explore," no preamble. The first line is the position.
2. **Why most people believe the opposite** (1–2 paragraphs) — Steelman the conventional view fairly. Quote the people the founder is disagreeing with. Make it clear the founder understands the other side before disagreeing.
3. **Why the conventional view is wrong** (3–5 paragraphs) — The actual argument. Use evidence: customer quotes from `icp.md`, observations from competitive analysis, the founder's own experience. The strongest move here is a specific scenario that breaks the conventional model.
4. **What follows from being right** (1–2 paragraphs) — If the founder's position is correct, what does that imply? What should the reader do differently?
5. **Acknowledgment of edge cases** (1 paragraph, optional) — Where might the founder be wrong? Naming live questions builds credibility, not undermines it.
6. **CTA** — Usually subtle: read the next post, join the waitlist, email the founder.

**Length target:** 1200–1800 words.

## Format 2: The Deep-Dive Analysis

**Best for:** Distribution intent for a specific buyer audience. Posts where the founder wants to demonstrate expertise by going deeper than competitors do.

**When it works:** When the founder has access to data, case studies, or operational detail that competitors cannot match. When the post can credibly claim "no one else has written this in this depth."

**When it fails:** When the depth is just length without insight. When the post is exhaustive but not selective.

**Structural skeleton:**

1. **Hook with the question or finding** (1 paragraph) — What surprising thing did the founder discover or want to investigate? Make the question concrete.
2. **Why this question matters** (1 paragraph) — Stakes. Who cares about the answer and what hinges on it.
3. **What the field currently believes** (2–3 paragraphs) — A fair survey of the existing literature, vendor positioning, or analyst takes.
4. **The investigation** (5–8 paragraphs) — The actual analysis. Walk through the evidence step by step. Use bullet lists, tables, or worked examples where they clarify. This is where the founder earns the reader's time.
5. **The finding** (2–3 paragraphs) — What the analysis revealed, stated plainly. Often this is a partial finding with caveats — that is more credible than a clean answer.
6. **Implications** (1–2 paragraphs) — What should change because of this finding?
7. **Methodology note** (optional, 1 paragraph) — How the founder did the analysis, for readers who want to verify.

**Length target:** 1800–3000 words. Deep-dives are allowed to run long.

## Format 3: The Customer Story / Pattern Post

**Best for:** Distribution and proof. Posts that show the problem through a specific scenario.

**When it works:** When the founder has a real customer (or composite) whose situation illustrates the broader problem. When the story is specific enough to feel real but generalizable enough to apply to the reader.

**When it fails:** When the story is generic ("a SaaS company we work with..." with no specifics). When the moral of the story is forced. When the post feels like a case study disguised as a blog post.

**Structural skeleton:**

1. **Open with the moment of the problem** (1–2 paragraphs) — In medias res. The customer is in the middle of the pain. Use specific details: a name, a number, a quote. (Anonymized if needed; specifics matter more than identification.)
2. **Why this happened** (2–3 paragraphs) — The structural reason the customer ended up here. Connect it to a pattern, not just bad luck.
3. **What the customer tried** (2–3 paragraphs) — The workarounds, the half-solutions, the wrong tools. This is where the post earns the reader's empathy.
4. **What actually worked** (2–4 paragraphs) — The reframe, the tool, the approach that got the customer unstuck. This is the closest a customer story gets to a product pitch — keep it grounded in the customer's experience, not in the founder's product.
5. **Why this matters for other readers** (1–2 paragraphs) — Generalize the pattern. Who else has this problem and what should they take away.
6. **CTA** — Usually direct: "If this sounds like your team, here's the waitlist."

**Length target:** 1200–1800 words.

## Format 4: The Listicle (Used Sparingly)

**Best for:** SEO intent for query patterns like "X ways to..." or "best tools for..." or "things every [role] should know about [X]". Distribution intent for listicle-friendly social formats.

**When it works:** When the list is genuinely useful and each item earns its place. When the founder can defend each item against "would I add this to my own bookmarks?"

**When it fails:** When the list is padded to hit a number (the founder picked "10 ways to..." and only had 6 real ways). When the items are bland and interchangeable. When the post optimizes for the keyword and forgets the reader.

**Structural skeleton:**

1. **Hook that justifies the list** (1 paragraph) — Why this list, why now, who needs it.
2. **A note on selection** (1 paragraph, optional) — How the founder chose what made the list. This adds credibility and prevents "why isn't [X] on this list" complaints.
3. **The list** (one section per item, with H2 or H3 headers) — Each item:
   - Has a specific, descriptive title (not "Tool 1: Mindtickle" but "Mindtickle — for buyers who already have a content library and need to add light AI coaching")
   - Includes 1–2 sentences on what it is
   - Includes 1–2 sentences on when it is the right choice
   - Includes 1 sentence on when it is NOT the right choice (this is the move that distinguishes the post from a vendor's own marketing)
4. **A meta-observation** (1 paragraph) — What does the pattern across the items reveal about the category?
5. **CTA** — Subtle: read another post, join the waitlist.

**Length target:** 1200–2000 words.

**Warning:** Listicles are the easiest format to write and the easiest format to write badly. Use this format only when the listicle structure is the actual right answer for the post, not because the founder wants something easy to draft.

## Format 5: The Founder Essay

**Best for:** Founder POV intent. Posts that establish the founder's personal credibility and worldview, separate from the company.

**When it works:** When the founder has a real story, real expertise, or a real opinion worth taking up space for. When the essay is in first person and admits uncertainty where appropriate.

**When it fails:** When the essay is performative thought leadership. When it is a thinly disguised company pitch. When the founder is writing for other founders rather than for the buyer.

**Structural skeleton:**

1. **Personal anecdote or observation** (1–2 paragraphs) — A specific moment from the founder's experience that anchors the essay. Use first person. Use specifics.
2. **What the moment made the founder realize** (1–2 paragraphs) — The insight or question that emerged.
3. **The deeper pattern** (3–5 paragraphs) — Generalize from the specific to the structural. Bring in evidence from work, research, or conversations with customers.
4. **Where the founder is still wrong** (1 paragraph) — Live question, edge case, thing the founder has not figured out yet. This is the move that distinguishes essays from sermons.
5. **What this means for the reader** (1 paragraph) — Light-touch implication. Founder essays are not how-tos; they should leave the reader thinking, not acting.

**Length target:** 1000–1800 words. Founder essays are usually shorter than analytical posts.

**Voice note:** Founder essays are the place where the founder's personal voice diverges from the company brand voice. The company is allowed to be more guarded; the founder is allowed to be more uncertain. Reference `founder-profile.md` for the founder's actual voice, not just `brand-voice.md`.

## Format 6: The Launch / Announcement Post

**Best for:** Product/launch intent. Posts that mark a milestone — product launch, fundraise, hire, partnership, milestone.

**When it works:** When the post is honest about what is being launched and what is not yet ready. When the founder grounds the launch in the customer problem rather than the company achievement.

**When it fails:** When the post is corporate boilerplate. When it leads with the founder's excitement rather than the customer's reason to care.

**Structural skeleton:**

1. **The customer problem in one sentence** (1 paragraph) — Restate what is broken in the world. Use ICP vocabulary.
2. **What we are launching** (2–3 paragraphs) — Specifically what is being announced. Show, do not tell — screenshots, examples, concrete capabilities.
3. **Why we built it this way** (2–3 paragraphs) — The non-obvious decisions. The thing that distinguishes this launch from the obvious version of the same thing.
4. **What is not yet ready** (1 paragraph) — The honest list. Adds credibility.
5. **Who this is for** (1 paragraph) — The ICP, named explicitly. Not "everyone."
6. **What is next** (1 paragraph) — The roadmap, in broad strokes.
7. **CTA** — Direct. Sign up, request access, email the founder.

**Length target:** 800–1500 words. Launch posts can be shorter than other formats — get to the point.

---

## How To Choose Between Formats

| Intent | Best format | Second choice |
|---|---|---|
| SEO target | Deep-dive analysis | Listicle (only if the SERP is listicle-dominated) |
| Distribution | Customer story / pattern post | Opinionated POV |
| Founder POV | Founder essay | Opinionated POV |
| Product/launch | Launch post | Customer story (when the launch is grounded in a specific user) |

If the founder cannot decide between two formats, ask: "Which version would you actually want to read?"
