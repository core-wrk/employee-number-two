# Research Question Framework

This is an internal reference for the `research-planner` skill. It defines what makes a strong vs. weak research question and provides examples. **Do not show this document to the founder.** Use it to calibrate when to push back and when to accept a question as specific enough to act on.

---

## What Makes a Research Question Strong

A strong research question has four properties:

1. **Specific.** It names a particular thing, not a category. "Do sales reps complain about Lessonly's content staleness in the last 12 months?" is specific. "How do people feel about sales enablement tools?" is not.
2. **Decision-linked.** The answer would change a decision the founder is about to make. If the answer to the question does not move any decision, the question is diligence for its own sake.
3. **Falsifiable.** It is possible to imagine an answer that would disprove the founder's working belief. A question the founder would answer the same way regardless of evidence is not a research question — it is a prayer.
4. **Time-bound where relevant.** For fast-moving topics (pricing, product launches, company status), the question specifies the time window. "Has Gong's pricing changed in the last 12 months?" is bounded. "What is Gong's pricing?" is not.

## Strong vs. Weak Examples

### Topic: Problem validation

**Strong:**
- "Do mid-market SaaS sales managers publicly complain about rep ramp time in the last 12 months, and if so, what specific failures do they describe?"
- "When a sales leader describes onboarding a new rep, which of the 5 pain points in my hypothesis do they mention unprompted?"

**Weak:**
- "Is rep ramp time a real problem?" (not falsifiable — any answer becomes "yes, see anecdotes")
- "How do sales teams feel about onboarding?" (no decision link, no specificity)

---

### Topic: Competitive landscape

**Strong:**
- "Do Lessonly, Mindtickle, and Brainshark currently support call-recording ingestion, and if so, at what pricing tier?"
- "Which of the top 5 sales enablement platforms has lost customers to cheaper point tools in the last 12 months, based on public signals?"

**Weak:**
- "Who are the competitors?" (first step, not a research question)
- "What is the competitive landscape like?" (too broad to search)

---

### Topic: Customer buying behavior

**Strong:**
- "When a VP of Sales Enablement writes publicly about evaluating a new tool, what 3 criteria come up most often in the last 18 months?"
- "What is the typical budget range a mid-market company allocates to sales training tools per seat per month?"

**Weak:**
- "How do customers decide what to buy?" (no anchor to a specific role or context)
- "What do buyers care about?" (no scope, no decision link)

---

### Topic: Market momentum / why now

**Strong:**
- "Have there been any funding rounds larger than $10M in the AI-for-sales-training category in the last 12 months, and what did the companies position around?"
- "Has the average sales rep tenure at mid-market SaaS companies shifted in the last 3 years based on public data?"

**Weak:**
- "Is the market growing?" (every market is 'growing' by someone's measure)
- "Is now the right time?" (not testable)

## Pushback Prompts to Use in Conversation

When the founder offers a vague research question, use one of these to push toward specificity. Vary them — do not use the same one twice in a session.

- "If you got the answer to that, what decision would it change?"
- "What would you need to see for the answer to be 'no'?"
- "Can you name a specific person or company that might embody the answer?"
- "What is the shortest version of that question you could actually search for?"
- "Is there a time window on that — last month, last year, ever?"

---

## When to Stop Pushing

If after one or two pushes the founder is still giving a fuzzy answer, stop pushing and accept the fuzzy version. Write it down as-is and flag it in the plan as "exploratory." Not every research question has to be sharp — some are genuinely open-ended. The goal of pushback is to expose the questions where the founder is avoiding specificity, not to force every question into a rigid format.

A good rule: if you have pushed twice and the answer still resists sharpening, that itself is signal. The founder may not yet know what they are looking for, and the right move is to pick a tangential question that will help them discover it.

---

## Target Count

Aim for **3–5 research questions** in the final plan. Fewer than 3 and the plan is probably missing important angles. More than 5 and the founder will not actually run all of them — better to have 4 sharp questions than 8 mediocre ones.

If the founder insists on more than 5, that is fine — let them. Do not enforce a cap artificially.
