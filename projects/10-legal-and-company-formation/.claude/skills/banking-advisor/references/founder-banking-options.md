# Founder Banking Options — Reference

**Scope:** US business banking providers commonly used by early-stage startups and small businesses. Covers modern neobanks, traditional banks, and credit unions. Providers listed are widely used; inclusion is not endorsement.

**Not legal or financial advice.** This reference summarizes tradeoffs to inform a comparison, not to make a decision. Providers change features, pricing, and eligibility frequently — verify current offerings on their sites before opening an account.

---

## Post-SVB Context

Silicon Valley Bank failed on March 10, 2023. The impact still shapes the landscape:

- **Multi-institution diversification became standard.** Any company holding more than FDIC coverage ($250k per institution per depositor category) now typically maintains accounts at two or more institutions. Many VCs require this contractually.
- **FDIC sweep programs became competitive differentiators.** Modern neobanks partner with networks of FDIC-insured banks to offer sweep coverage far above $250k (Mercury's Vault: up to $5M; Brex: up to $6M). This works by splitting deposits across multiple member banks automatically.
- **Treasury yield became a real cost consideration.** With risk-free rates above 4–5% for an extended period, idle cash sitting at 0% in a checking account has a meaningful opportunity cost. Most providers now offer money-market or treasury yield programs.
- **VC portfolio banking mandates became more common.** VCs often publish required or preferred banking lists for portfolio companies after the SVB collapse exposed single-provider concentration risk.

## Mercury

**Who it serves:** US startups (funded or bootstrapped), tech-forward small businesses, international founders with US entities.

**Strengths:**
- No monthly fees, no minimum balance
- No personal credit check for account opening
- Strong developer API; native integrations with QuickBooks, Xero, Stripe, accounting tools
- Mercury Vault: FDIC sweep up to $5M across partner banks
- Mercury Treasury: money-market fund yield, currently in the 4–5% range (verify current)
- Excellent international wire support; competitive FX
- VC-comfort is strong (widely used across startup portfolios)

**Weaknesses:**
- No physical branches; fully online
- Cash deposits via third-party network (Allpoint+) with fees and friction — not good for cash-heavy businesses
- Lending products limited (recent expansion into credit cards and credit lines but not a full commercial lending bank)
- Had a brief period of post-SVB tightening on which international founders could open accounts; now relaxed but verify

**Best for:** The modern default for US-based funded or fundraising-bound startups. Also works well for bootstrapped solo founders who want zero friction.

---

## Brex

**Who it serves:** Funded startups, higher-balance companies, spend-heavy operations.

**Strengths:**
- Cash Management with FDIC sweep up to $6M
- Integrated corporate card with strong rewards and spend controls
- Travel and expense management built in
- Real-time treasury yield
- Strong for expense-heavy operations (global payroll integrations, receipt capture, per-trip controls)
- No personal guarantee required on corporate card

**Weaknesses:**
- Pivoted in 2022 to focus on funded companies; bootstrapped or pre-revenue companies often do not qualify for the full product suite
- Account opening can be slower than Mercury
- Historically had some customer-service volatility during 2022–2023 product pivots

**Best for:** Funded startups with meaningful spend volume who want cards, travel management, and banking in one provider. Less compelling for solo bootstrapped founders.

## Chase Business / Bank of America Business / Wells Fargo Business

**Who they serve:** Broadly — small businesses of all types. Most common choice outside the tech-startup bubble.

**Strengths:**
- Physical branches everywhere; cash deposits trivial
- Established lending relationships — business credit cards, lines of credit, SBA loans available
- Nationally recognized by any vendor, customer, or partner
- If the founder already banks personally with Chase or BofA, consolidated login and relationship management is convenient
- Treasury products for larger balances

**Weaknesses:**
- Personal credit check (soft or hard depending on product) during account opening
- Typically charge monthly fees unless minimum balance is maintained (Chase Business Complete: $15/month unless $2k average balance; others similar)
- Integrations with modern accounting tools require exports, not native connections
- Wire fees higher than neobanks (~$25–$35 outgoing domestic; $45+ international)
- Treasury yield programs exist but typically require relationship-manager engagement and higher balances

**Best for:** Bootstrapped founders who already bank there personally and want one login, cash-heavy businesses that need branch deposits, founders who anticipate needing SBA or traditional lending.

## Relay

**Who it serves:** Bootstrapped small businesses, operators who want multiple accounts for budgeting (e.g., Profit First methodology).

**Strengths:**
- No fees on base product
- Allows up to 20 sub-accounts for operational budgeting
- Strong integrations with QuickBooks and Xero
- No personal credit check
- FDIC insured through partner Thread Bank

**Weaknesses:**
- Smaller brand; VC-comfort lower than Mercury or Brex (not a deal-breaker but worth noting)
- International wire support less developed than Mercury
- No treasury yield program (as of mid-2024)

**Best for:** Bootstrapped operators who want Profit-First-style account separation and zero fees.

---

## Bluevine

**Who it serves:** Small businesses and e-commerce operators.

**Strengths:**
- 2% APY on balances up to a cap (verify current)
- Lending products integrated (lines of credit, invoice factoring)
- No monthly fees, no minimum balance

**Weaknesses:**
- Less startup-focused than Mercury or Brex
- Account-opening process more document-heavy than neobank peers

**Best for:** Revenue-generating small businesses that want interest-bearing checking and access to working-capital lending.

---

## Local Credit Unions

**Who it serves:** Very small operations, bootstrapped solo founders, local service businesses.

**Strengths:**
- Often lowest fees of any option
- Strong personal relationships; lending decisions made by humans who know you
- Not-for-profit; profits returned to members in better rates

**Weaknesses:**
- VC-comfort: essentially zero. Investors will push for a move to Mercury or equivalent if fundraising happens.
- Technology stack often dated (mobile apps, integrations, API access)
- International wire support varies wildly by credit union

**Best for:** Bootstrapped operators who never plan to raise institutional capital and value personal banking relationships over tech features.

---

## Wise Business (formerly TransferWise)

**Who it serves:** Companies with heavy international operations.

**Strengths:**
- Multi-currency accounts (hold balances in USD, GBP, EUR, and dozens of others simultaneously)
- Mid-market exchange rates for FX (materially cheaper than bank FX markups)
- Fast international transfers at transparent fees

**Weaknesses:**
- Not a full-service bank; limited domestic US features
- Treasury yield limited
- Typically used as a secondary account for FX, not a primary operating account

**Best for:** Secondary account for companies with international contractors, customers, or operations.

## Provider Matchmaking

| Situation | Primary | Secondary (if $250k+) |
|---|---|---|
| VC-bound US startup (default) | Mercury | Brex or Chase Business |
| Funded, spend-heavy operations | Brex | Mercury |
| Bootstrapped solo founder | Mercury or Relay | — (likely below $250k) |
| Bootstrapped, cash-heavy or local | Local credit union or Chase | Mercury |
| International-heavy operations | Mercury (primary) + Wise (secondary for FX) | — |
| Existing Chase/BofA customer, bootstrapped | Chase Business | — |

## Account-Opening Document Checklist

Most providers will require:

- **Certificate of Incorporation or Articles of Organization** (from your state Secretary of State filing)
- **EIN confirmation letter** (Form CP-575 from the IRS, or Form 147C if CP-575 is lost — request by calling IRS Business & Specialty line)
- **Operating Agreement (LLC) or Bylaws (corporation)**
- **Board/Member resolution authorizing account opening** (some providers require this; template usually provided)
- **Government-issued photo ID** for every beneficial owner with 25%+ ownership (FinCEN requirement under Corporate Transparency Act / Beneficial Ownership Information rule)
- **Proof of address** for beneficial owners (utility bill, lease, etc.)

Non-US founders opening a US account also typically need an ITIN or SSN for at least one signatory, plus passport for ID.

## Corporate Cards

Most banking providers now have integrated card programs:

- **Brex Card:** Deep spend controls, global acceptance, strong rewards (~2x points on most categories); requires funded or revenue-generating company
- **Mercury IO Credit Card:** Launched 2024; integrated with Mercury banking; more accessible than Brex for smaller companies
- **Ramp Card:** Charge card with exceptional spend management and automated expense controls; requires revenue or funding threshold
- **Divvy (Bill Spend & Expense):** Expense-management focus; more common at established SMBs than startups
- **Traditional bank business cards (Chase Ink, Amex Business):** Require personal guarantee and good personal credit; rewards can be strong (Ink Preferred: 3x on travel and common business categories)

Card approval is separate from account opening. Brex and Ramp in particular have eligibility criteria (typical thresholds: funded startup with $50k+ in bank, or $3M+ ARR for revenue path). Mercury IO is more accessible.

## Things That Change Often

These are worth verifying on provider websites before recommending:

- Treasury/yield rates
- FDIC sweep coverage amounts
- Monthly fee structures
- International wire fees
- Integration partnerships (new platforms, removed ones)
- Card program eligibility thresholds

A reference file that bakes in specific rates will be wrong within 3 months. Recommend, anchor on category (e.g., "4–5% range, verify current"), and point to provider rate pages.
