# Voice Attribute Examples

This is an internal reference for the `brand-voice-developer` skill. It shows what well-defined voice attributes look like in practice, drawn from a small set of brands whose voices are widely recognized as distinctive. **Do not show this document to the founder.** Use it to (a) recognize when a founder's proposed voice attribute is too abstract to be useful, and (b) model the structure of good attribute definitions in the brand voice guide. Do not let the founder copy any of these brands directly — the examples are illustrative of *form*, not templates to imitate.

---

## What makes a voice attribute useful

A useful voice attribute has four ingredients:

1. **A short label** (one or two words) that the founder can hold in their head
2. **An operational definition** specific to *this* brand, not a dictionary definition
3. **A concrete example** of what the attribute sounds like in writing
4. **An anti-pattern** — what the attribute does NOT sound like

If any of the four is missing, the attribute is too abstract to guide downstream writing.

Bad attribute (from the wild): "Authentic." — Useless. Every brand says it is authentic.

Good attribute: "Anti-marketing-language." — "We say what we mean, in the words our customers actually use. If a sentence could appear on any SaaS homepage, we rewrite it." Sounds like: "Most sales onboarding fails because the AI feedback is generic." Does NOT sound like: "We empower revenue teams with next-generation enablement solutions."

---

## Example brands

The four brands below are referenced because their voices are distinctive enough to study, and their attribute structures are recognizably similar to what the brand voice guide should produce. Use them as form references, not as templates.

---

### Mailchimp — "Plain, helpful, slightly playful"

**Audience:** Creative SMB owners and marketers who do not want to feel talked down to or condescended to by enterprise software.

**Voice attributes:**

1. **Plain.** Use the most direct word for the thing. "Send" instead of "deploy." "List" instead of "audience segmentation cohort." Sounds like: "Make your campaign live whenever you're ready." Does NOT sound like: "Activate your strategic engagement workflow."

2. **Helpful, not hand-holding.** Explain enough to let the user move forward, but trust them to figure things out. Sounds like: "Your campaign is scheduled. We'll send it Tuesday at 9 AM." Does NOT sound like: "Congratulations on completing this step! In the next exciting phase, you will discover how to..."

3. **Lightly playful, never goofy.** A small visual joke or a wink in the copy is OK if it does not get in the way of the task. Sounds like: "Looks like a few subscribers bounced. Happens to all of us." Does NOT sound like: "Oops! Some emails went bye-bye! 😢"

**Why it works for Mailchimp:** SMB owners are intimidated by enterprise software vocabulary. The plain-and-helpful voice removes that intimidation. The light playfulness signals that the brand has personality without being a clown.

**Why it would NOT work for the example founder's brand:** Mailchimp's audience wants to feel relaxed. The example founder's audience (skeptical RevOps and enablement leaders evaluating a $100–200/seat product) wants to feel that the founder takes the problem seriously. Lightly playful would read as unserious here.

---

### Stripe — "Precise, calm, deeply technical when it matters"

**Audience:** Engineers and product builders who are evaluating payment infrastructure and care about reliability, edge cases, and getting the details right.

**Voice attributes:**

1. **Precise.** Every claim is specific. Numbers are real. Edge cases are named. Sounds like: "We process payments in 135 currencies and settle in 47." Does NOT sound like: "We support payments globally."

2. **Calm.** No hype, no urgency manufactured by the copy. The tone is the tone of someone who knows what they are doing and is not selling against fear. Sounds like: "Stripe's API has a 99.999% uptime SLA." Does NOT sound like: "Don't let payment failures destroy your business — switch to Stripe today!"

3. **Technical when the reader is technical, plain when the reader is not.** Documentation is precise and detailed; landing pages are plain. Sounds like (docs): "POST /v1/charges accepts a `source` parameter that can be a card, bank account, or saved customer." Sounds like (landing): "Accept payments from any country, with one integration."

**Why it works for Stripe:** Engineers can smell hype. The calm-precise voice signals that Stripe is built by people who respect engineers and do not need to oversell.

**Why it would NOT work directly for the example founder:** Stripe's audience cares about technical accuracy more than emotional resonance. The example founder's audience (sales enablement leaders) cares about both — they want the precision but they also want to know the founder understands the human side of sales onboarding. A pure Stripe voice would feel too cold.

**Useful piece to borrow:** The calm-precise stance is right for skeptical RevOps buyers. The example founder's voice can lean toward calm and precise without being as dry as Stripe.

---

### Linear — "Confident, spare, slightly opinionated"

**Audience:** Software engineering teams that have used Jira and want something better. Sophisticated, opinionated, value taste and craft.

**Voice attributes:**

1. **Confident.** Linear takes positions. The website is full of statements like "Built for the way modern software is made." This is a claim, not a hedge. Sounds like: "Linear is built for the way modern software is made." Does NOT sound like: "Linear is one of many tools you might consider for project management."

2. **Spare.** Few words. Lots of white space. The opposite of Atlassian's wall of features. Sounds like: "Issues. Cycles. Roadmaps." Does NOT sound like: "Comprehensive issue tracking, agile cycle management, and strategic roadmap visualization across teams."

3. **Slightly opinionated.** Linear is willing to say what it thinks is wrong with other tools, by implication if not by name. Sounds like: "Built for product teams who care about craft." Does NOT sound like: "Suitable for any team using any methodology."

**Why it works for Linear:** Their audience is opinionated themselves. They respect a tool that has a point of view. A wishy-washy Linear would lose them.

**Useful piece to borrow:** The "willing to take positions" stance maps well to a challenger positioning. If the example founder is positioning against Mindtickle and Gong, the willingness to be opinionated about what is wrong with the existing tools is a useful voice ingredient. But: the spareness is harder to borrow — RevOps buyers usually want more substance and proof than Linear's homepage offers.

---

### Basecamp / 37signals — "Plainspoken, opinionated, slightly contrarian"

**Audience:** Small-team founders and managers who are skeptical of enterprise software bloat and value simplicity.

**Voice attributes:**

1. **Plainspoken.** Conversational. Reads like a person wrote it, not a marketing department. Sounds like: "We've been doing this for 20 years. Here's what we learned." Does NOT sound like: "Two decades of leadership in the project management category."

2. **Opinionated.** Basecamp will tell you that you do not need most of the features you think you need. Sounds like: "You don't need real-time chat. You need fewer interruptions." Does NOT sound like: "Includes real-time chat, video calling, asynchronous messaging, and 47 other communication tools."

3. **Slightly contrarian.** Basecamp explicitly positions against the rest of the category. The contrarianism is the brand. Sounds like: "Nobody needs more software. Everybody needs better software." Does NOT sound like: "Best-in-class software for modern teams."

**Why it works for Basecamp:** Their audience is exhausted by software bloat. The contrarian voice is permission to opt out.

**Useful piece to borrow:** The willingness to be plainspoken and opinionated is portable. If the example founder is positioning against generic AI feedback, the contrarian stance ("most AI sales coaching is generic, ours is not") is a natural voice ingredient.

## How to use these examples in the conversation

In Phase 3 of the brand-voice-developer flow (define 3–5 voice attributes), use these brand examples to:

1. **Show the founder what good attribute definitions look like.** Read one or two of the attribute definitions out loud from this document (e.g., the Linear "Confident" or Stripe "Precise" definition) and ask: "Does this kind of structure work for what you want to define for your brand?"

2. **Push back when the founder's proposed attribute is too abstract.** If the founder says "trustworthy," reference Stripe's "Precise" — point out that "trustworthy" is what the audience concludes when the brand is precise. The brand should describe its behavior, not the audience's reaction.

3. **Help the founder pick a stance, not borrow a brand.** Do not let the founder say "we want to sound like Stripe." Stripe sounds the way it does because of its specific audience. Ask: "What would a brand sound like for an audience that is RevOps leaders evaluating sales onboarding tools? What does that audience respond to?"

4. **Use the "would NOT work for the example founder" notes** to help the founder avoid mismatches. Mailchimp's playful voice would alienate the example founder's audience. The mismatch is instructive.

## Common founder mistakes when defining voice

- **Picking adjectives that are universal.** "Authentic, transparent, customer-focused, innovative." Every brand claims these. They guide nothing.
- **Picking adjectives that contradict the audience.** Playful for an audience that wants gravitas. Spare for an audience that wants proof.
- **Picking adjectives that contradict the founder's actual writing.** A "warm and conversational" attribute for a founder who writes in short, blunt, slightly sarcastic sentences will not survive contact with the founder's actual output.
- **Trying to be everything.** "Confident but humble, playful but professional, technical but approachable." Pick a side. The brand voice can be confident OR humble more than it is the other; trying to be both equally produces nothing distinctive.
- **Copying a famous brand wholesale.** Linear's voice works for Linear. The founder's voice has to work for the founder's audience and the founder's actual writing style.

## Final test before finalizing voice attributes

Read the proposed attributes back to yourself and ask:

1. Could these attributes be used by 3+ other companies in this category? (If yes, too generic.)
2. Is each attribute paired with a concrete example sentence and an anti-pattern? (If no, too abstract.)
3. Does the founder's existing writing already lean in this direction, even slightly? (If no, the voice will not be sustainable.)
4. Does each attribute serve the positioning, or is it just about personality? (If just personality, the voice has no strategic purpose.)

If any of these answers is wrong, push the founder to revise before moving to Phase 4.
