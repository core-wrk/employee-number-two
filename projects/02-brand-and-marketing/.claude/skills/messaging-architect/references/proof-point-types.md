# Proof Point Types

This is an internal reference for the `messaging-architect` skill. It defines the categories of proof points that can support a message pillar, ranked by credibility, with notes on when each type fits and when it does not. **Do not show this document to the founder.** Use it to push the founder toward the most credible proof type available, and to flag when a proof point is weaker than it sounds.

---

## Why proof points matter

A pillar without a proof point is a claim. A claim without a proof point is marketing language. Marketing language without proof is what makes early-stage founders sound like they are pitching from a deck instead of from evidence.

The job of a proof point is to give the buyer a reason to believe the pillar is true *for them*. The strongest proof points are specific, attributable, and recent. The weakest are generic, unattributable, and timeless.

---

## The seven proof point types, ranked by credibility

### 1. Quantified customer outcome (most credible)

**What it looks like:** A specific, named customer achieving a specific, measured result.

**Examples:**
- "Three reps at [Customer X] cut their first-call confidence rating from 4/10 to 8/10 in two weeks"
- "[Customer Y] reduced ramp time from 6 months to 3.5 months across their last hiring cohort"
- "[Customer Z] eliminated 30 hours per week of senior-rep shadow time"

**When it fits:**
- After Project 08 (beta and user testing) has produced real customer outcomes
- For pillars about value or business impact
- For buyer-facing messaging where ROI matters

**When it does NOT fit:**
- Pre-Project 08 — there are no customer outcomes yet, so this is a LIVE QUESTION proof point at best
- For pillars about user experience or product feel — those need different proof types
- When the customer who achieved the result will not let you name them — anonymized quantified outcomes ("a 250-rep B2B SaaS company") are weaker than named ones, but still stronger than no proof

**Founder pitfall:** Inventing quantified outcomes from thin air or extrapolating from a single rep. If the proof is hypothetical, mark it as LIVE QUESTION and move on.

---

### 2. Verbatim user voice with attribution

**What it looks like:** A direct quote from a real user, named or anonymized but with provenance.

**Examples:**
- "'I finally feel like I know what to say when prospects push back on price' — Sarah, AE at [Customer X], week 3 of onboarding"
- "'Mindtickle's quizzes don't translate to real conversations' — G2 review, March 2025"
- "'We've been doing shadow pairing for years and it just doesn't scale' — VP Sales Enablement, anonymous interview, Project 01"

**When it fits:**
- When the quote names a specific moment, frustration, or insight (not a generic endorsement)
- For pillars where the customer's emotional response matters (user-facing pillars especially)
- When the source is verifiable — Project 01 research outputs, G2/Capterra reviews, public Reddit threads, customer interviews

**When it does NOT fit:**
- When the quote is generic ("It's great!" — useless)
- When the quote is a paraphrase of a paraphrase (use direct quotes only)
- When the source is "we heard from a customer" without provenance

**Founder pitfall:** Paraphrasing instead of quoting. The literal words the customer used are usually more credible than a sanitized rewrite. Quote verbatim wherever possible.

**Bridge to Project 01:** The example founder has a strong corpus of research-grounded user voices in `competitive-landscape.md` and `icp.md`. These can be reused as proof points for messaging pillars about gaps in the existing market.

---

### 3. Specific mechanism / how-it-works data point

**What it looks like:** A specific number or technical detail about how the product works that the customer can verify or that signals depth.

**Examples:**
- "Trained on 10,000+ closed-won and closed-lost deal transcripts per customer"
- "Pulls from 18 months of Gong call recordings on first sync"
- "Grades responses against the company's own 12-month win-rate baseline"

**When it fits:**
- For pillars about a unique attribute (the mechanism is the proof of the attribute)
- When the founder needs to demonstrate technical depth to a skeptical buyer
- When the customer is sophisticated enough to care about the mechanism (RevOps and enablement leaders usually are)

**When it does NOT fit:**
- For pillars about user experience or emotional value
- When the mechanism is hypothetical (mark as LIVE QUESTION)
- For non-technical buyers who do not care how it works

**Founder pitfall:** Using vague mechanism claims like "AI-powered" or "machine learning models." Specificity is the whole point — "trained on 10,000+ deal transcripts" is a proof point; "uses machine learning" is not.

---

### 4. Named-logo social proof

**What it looks like:** "Used by [Named Customer]" or a logo wall.

**Examples:**
- "Used by [Customer X], a Series C SaaS company onboarding 30 reps per quarter"
- "Trusted by enablement leaders at [Customer X], [Customer Y], and [Customer Z]"

**When it fits:**
- After at least 3–5 named customers have used the product and agreed to be referenced
- For buyer-facing messaging where peer signal matters
- For mid-to-late-stage messaging — early stage usually does not have logos yet

**When it does NOT fit:**
- Pre-revenue or pre-pilot — no logos to use, or only 1–2 logos which is weaker than no logos at all
- When the logo is from a customer who is not actually using the product successfully (the buyer will check)
- For user-facing messaging — users do not care which other companies bought it

**Founder pitfall:** Listing logos of "interested" companies, design partners that have not actually deployed, or one-time purchasers who churned. Logo walls are read by buyers who *will* check; padding the list damages credibility.

---

### 5. Founder credential / domain authority

**What it looks like:** A specific claim about the founder's experience that is relevant to the problem.

**Examples (drawn from `founder-profile.md`):**
- "Built by a former VP of RevOps with 6 years of experience leading sales onboarding at B2B SaaS companies"
- "Founder previously evaluated and rejected 3 of the existing tools in this space as a buyer"

**When it fits:**
- Early stage, when there are no customer outcomes or logos yet
- When the founder's credential is specifically relevant to the problem the product solves
- For credibility with sophisticated buyers who care that the founder has been in their seat

**When it does NOT fit:**
- When the credential is generic ("ex-FAANG engineer" for a product about sales onboarding — irrelevant)
- As a substitute for product proof — credential gets the buyer to take the meeting; product proof gets the buyer to sign
- When the founder's credential is legitimately thin (do not invent one)

**Founder pitfall:** Over-leaning on the credential to compensate for missing product proof. The credential opens doors; it does not close deals on its own.

---

### 6. Comparative claim

**What it looks like:** A direct comparison to a named competitor on a specific dimension.

**Examples:**
- "Unlike Mindtickle, the AI feedback is grounded in your own deal history"
- "Unlike Second Nature, the simulated buyers are based on your real customers, not generic personas"

**When it fits:**
- When the comparison is specific and verifiable (the buyer could check)
- When the differentiator is one the buyer already cares about (look in `competitive-landscape.md` for the user-reported gaps on the named competitor)
- For pillars that are explicitly differentiation pillars

**When it does NOT fit:**
- When the comparison is vague ("better than Mindtickle" — better at what?)
- When the comparison is unfair (comparing your best feature to their weakest, when they have stronger features elsewhere — sophisticated buyers see through this)
- As the only proof type — comparative claims work best alongside customer outcomes or user voices

**Founder pitfall:** Comparative claims that the competitor would dispute. Read `competitive-landscape.md` carefully — if the user reviews complain about a specific gap, that is fair territory for a comparative claim. If the gap is the founder's interpretation rather than user-reported, the comparison is shakier.

---

### 7. Demo moment / experiential proof (least credible as a written proof, most credible in person)

**What it looks like:** A description of a specific moment in the product that demonstrates the claim.

**Examples:**
- "In the demo, paste in any objection your reps actually heard last quarter — the system grades a sample response against what worked in your real closed-won deals"
- "Show a new rep their first call's grading 60 seconds after they finish role-playing"

**When it fits:**
- For pillars about user experience or product feel
- As a "see for yourself" prompt that turns skepticism into a demo request
- When the demo can actually deliver what is described (the buyer will check)

**When it does NOT fit:**
- As a primary proof point in written messaging — demo moments are most credible when actually demonstrated, not described
- When the demo described does not actually work yet (pre-MVP)

**Founder pitfall:** Describing a demo moment that the product cannot actually do. This is the fastest way to lose a deal in the demo itself.

## How to choose proof points

For each pillar, the messaging-architect should ask:

1. What is the strongest type of proof available *right now* for this pillar?
2. If the strongest type is unavailable (e.g., no customer outcomes yet because Project 08 has not run), what is the next strongest?
3. Is the proof point honest about its status (VALIDATED / DIRECTIONALLY SUPPORTED / LIVE QUESTION)?

**General hierarchy at the example founder's stage** (post-Project 01, pre-Project 08):

- Quantified customer outcomes: usually NOT available yet → LIVE QUESTION
- Verbatim user voices from research: AVAILABLE — `competitive-landscape.md` and `icp.md` have many
- Specific mechanism data points: AVAILABLE for the parts of the product that are real
- Named-logo social proof: usually NOT available yet
- Founder credential: AVAILABLE — `founder-profile.md` has a strong, relevant credential
- Comparative claims: AVAILABLE based on `competitive-landscape.md`
- Demo moments: depends on what actually exists in the product

So a typical Project 02 messaging framework will lean heavily on (2) verbatim user voices from research, (5) founder credential, and (6) comparative claims, with (1) and (4) marked as LIVE QUESTION pending Project 08.

## Common mistakes

- **Treating "trusted by leading companies" as a proof point.** It is not. Name the companies or do not make the claim.
- **Using marketing-language as proof.** "Industry-leading," "best-in-class," "next-generation" are not proof.
- **Mixing strong and weak proof in the same pillar.** A pillar with one quantified outcome and one "customers love it" line reads weaker than the strong proof alone. Cut the weak one.
- **Pre-claiming Project 08 outcomes.** "Reps cut ramp time by 50%" pre-validation is a lie. Mark it as LIVE QUESTION or do not use it.
- **Using a single proof type for every pillar.** Variety in proof types makes the framework feel grounded; a list of three founder-credential proof points feels thin.

Push the founder toward 2–3 proof points per pillar, drawn from at least 2 different types where possible.
