# Funding Stage Guide

A working reference for `funding-landscape-researcher`. Covers the menu of funding instruments a US-based startup can realistically access, organized by stage, with tradeoffs, timelines, and red flags. Not exhaustive — deliberately scoped to the ~95% of situations early-stage founders encounter.

Verify current program specifics (batch deadlines, check-size ranges, active funds) via `web-researcher` at run time. Fund sizes and accelerator terms rot fast.

---

## 1. Stage definitions

**Pre-seed** — idea through prototype. No revenue or very early revenue. Team of 1–3. Typical round: $250K–$1.5M. Questions answered with this capital: can we build a prototype, can we get 3–5 design partners, can we find early product-market fit signal.

**Seed** — prototype through early traction. Signal of demand (LOIs, paid pilots, early MRR). Typical round: $1.5M–$5M (classic seed) or $3M–$8M (large seed / pre-A). Questions answered: can we reach PMF, can we get to $500K–$2M ARR, can we prove repeatable acquisition.

**Post-seed bridge** — between seed and Series A. Often a SAFE or extension round when Series A metrics haven't materialized. Typical round: $500K–$2M. Signal: you have some traction but the A isn't ready. Risk: bridge-to-nowhere if metrics don't move.

**Series A** — proven PMF, demonstrated repeatable GTM, targeting scale. Typical round: $8M–$25M. Metrics bar (varies by sector): $1–3M ARR, strong net retention, CAC payback < 18 months. Out of scope for most first-time Project 11 runs but mentioned for context.

## 3. Per-instrument deep dives

### Friends & family SAFE
- **Best fit:** earliest days, founder needs to live while building.
- **Typical terms:** post-money SAFE, $3–8M cap, no discount or 10–20% discount.
- **Timeline:** hours to days if lawyer template is ready.
- **Effort cost:** low, but social cost is real — if it doesn't work, these people lose money they can't afford to lose.
- **Signal:** none useful to later investors.
- **Watch for:** accredited-investor rules. Non-accredited F&F is legally messy; use a 506(b) exemption and document the relationship.

### Angel SAFE
- **Best fit:** pre-seed, founder has network of operators who've exited or senior operators in the ICP domain.
- **Typical terms:** post-money SAFE, $5–12M cap. Sophisticated angels push for MFN.
- **Timeline:** days to a few weeks per check; assembling a round of 8–15 angels takes 2–4 months.
- **Effort cost:** high — angel rounds are many small conversations, not one big one.
- **Signal:** the names on the cap table signal to the next round. Aim for 2–3 brand-name angels, not 15 unknowns.

### Scout check
- **What it is:** an angel-sized check written out of a scout fund (e.g. Sequoia Scouts, First Round Angel Track). The scout is usually a founder or operator; the VC stays invisible but knows.
- **Best fit:** pre-seed when you want VC-adjacent signal without the VC commitment.
- **Signal:** medium-high. The downstream fund is now aware of you and will often watch progress before their seed partner engages.
- **Watch for:** information rights. Scouts sometimes feed the parent fund data you might not have chosen to share.

### Syndicate (AngelList lead)
- **What it is:** a single lead (a named individual with a following) pools capital from LPs (often founders and operators) into a SPV per deal.
- **Best fit:** pre-seed or small seed when you have a compelling angel lead but need aggregate capital.
- **Typical structure:** lead takes 20% carry on the SPV. Deal-by-deal, not fund commitment.
- **Speed:** 2–6 weeks. Lead has to run their syndicate process.
- **Watch for:** signalling. A lead who stops leading (or whose syndicate under-fills) is visible to later investors.

### Accelerator
- **Three tiers:** YC (tier 1, national brand), Techstars / Alchemist / sector-specific (tier 2), regional / university (tier 3).
- **Terms:** varies. YC current terms: $500K on post-money SAFE ($125K for 7% + $375K uncapped MFN). Techstars: $120K for 6%.
- **Effort cost:** 3 months of full-time program participation for top-tier; partial commitment for others.
- **Signal:** tier-1 accelerator acceptance is often the single highest-signal credential a pre-seed company can earn.
- **Verify at run time:** current batch deadlines and program structure via `web-researcher`.

### Rolling fund
- **What it is:** a fund GP raises capital on a quarterly subscription basis. Writes checks on a rolling basis.
- **Best fit:** pre-seed and early seed with GP whose thesis matches yours.
- **Speed:** fast. Rolling-fund GPs are typically solo and can decide in 1–2 meetings.
- **Signal:** varies with GP. Some rolling funds have higher signal than traditional seed funds.

### Pre-seed micro-VC
- **What it is:** $5–25M funds focused on writing first institutional checks.
- **Best fit:** classic pre-seed where you want institutional infrastructure (quarterly updates, help hiring, downstream intros) at first-check stage.
- **Watch for:** board seat. Some micro-VCs take board seats at pre-seed; most don't. Ask.

### Sector-specialist seed
- **What it is:** $50–200M seed funds concentrated in one or two theses (fintech, dev tools, healthtech, climate).
- **Best fit:** seed round when your ICP and product fit their thesis.
- **Signal:** high. Sector-specialist backing tells downstream generalists the thesis is real.
- **Watch for:** portfolio conflicts. Sector specialists see many similar companies; they likely have 1–2 adjacent bets already.

### Generalist seed
- **What it is:** $100M+ seed funds investing across sectors.
- **Best fit:** seed when your story is big-tent and cross-vertical.
- **Board seat norm:** yes, usually. Expect formal governance from this stage.

### Corporate / strategic
- **What it is:** investment from a potential customer, partner, or competitor.
- **Best fit:** post-seed or A, when a strategic relationship would unlock distribution or data.
- **Watch for:** exclusivity clauses ("right of first refusal on future rounds," "must not sell to our competitors"), board observer seats that leak info, long decision timelines.
- **Signal:** mixed. Strategic money with good terms is great; with bad terms it locks you out of bigger outcomes.

### Revenue-based financing (RBF)
- **What it is:** capital repaid as a fixed percentage of future revenue until a multiple (1.3–1.8x) is reached.
- **Best fit:** predictable subscription revenue, $10K+ MRR, gross margins >60%. Capital for marketing / hiring, not for building.
- **Speed:** 2–6 weeks if you have clean Stripe data.
- **Not fundraising:** does not signal to VCs. Complement, not substitute.
- **Watch for:** covenant triggers, personal guarantees, revenue definitions.

### Federal grants
- **SBIR/STTR** (Small Business Innovation Research / Technology Transfer): Phase I ($50K–$300K, 6–12 months); Phase II ($750K–$2M, 2 years). Agencies: DoD, NIH, NSF, NASA, DOE, USDA.
- **NSF direct** (beyond SBIR): America's Seed Fund tracks.
- **Best fit:** deep-tech, hard-science, dual-use defense, climate.
- **Realistic timeline:** 6–12 months from submission to award. Plan accordingly.
- **Effort cost:** high. Grant writing is a specialized skill; 40–80 hours per submission.
- **Watch for:** matching-fund requirements, IP rights (SBIR IP stays with you; some state programs don't).

### State / sector grants
- **Examples:** Mass Life Sciences, NY Empire State Development, California IBank, sector programs (climate, AI, bio).
- **Realistic check size:** $10K–$250K typical.
- **Best fit:** supplements, not replacements. Good for buying runway during a grant-plus-angel pre-seed.

### Customer prepay
- **What it is:** a customer pays 12–24 months of contract up front, often at a small discount.
- **Why it's underrated:** strongest possible signal (they're paying you), zero dilution, zero investor management.
- **Best fit:** enterprise/mid-market deals where the customer has budget and is willing to pre-commit.
- **Watch for:** delivery risk. You've taken cash for a service you haven't delivered; scope creep is the failure mode.

### Reg CF crowdfunding
- **What it is:** equity crowdfunding under Regulation Crowdfunding ($5M cap per year). Platforms: Republic, Wefunder, StartEngine.
- **Best fit:** consumer / D2C brands with passionate users. Not suitable for most B2B SaaS.
- **Effort cost:** high. Running a campaign is a full marketing effort for 3–6 months.
- **Cap table implications:** hundreds or thousands of small shareholders. Some platforms pool these into a single SPV; confirm structure.

## 4. Realistic non-dilutive timelines

Non-dilutive capital sounds free but costs time.

| Source | Submission to decision | Decision to cash |
|---|---|---|
| SBIR Phase I | 6–9 months | 1–3 months |
| SBIR Phase II | 9–12 months | 1–3 months |
| NSF Direct | 9–12 months | 1–3 months |
| State grants | 2–4 months | 1–2 months |
| Sector grants | 3–6 months | 1–3 months |
| RBF (with clean data) | 2–4 weeks | 1–2 weeks |
| Customer prepay | Negotiation-dependent | Days to weeks |

If runway is < 9 months, treat grants as supplementary and raise equity as primary path.

## 5. Accelerator landscape snapshot

**Verify via `web-researcher` at run time** — batches and terms change.

**Tier 1 (national brand, competitive, meaningful signal):**
- Y Combinator — quarterly batches, ~1.5% acceptance, $500K current terms
- Techstars — city/vertical tracks, $120K + equity
- Alchemist — enterprise B2B focus

**Tier 2 (strong sector or regional reputation):**
- a16z Start — sector-specific
- On Deck (fellowships, not accelerators exactly)
- Entrepreneur First — talent-first model
- Sector examples: Launch (crypto), Plug and Play (varied), AngelPad

**Tier 3 (regional, university, corporate):**
- Mostly useful for network and modest capital. Do not expect meaningful signal lift.

## 6. Founder priority → instrument mapping

**Speed to close:**
1. Customer prepay (if the relationship exists)
2. Rolling fund / scout check
3. Angel SAFE with 1–2 leads

**Control (minimize board / governance overhead):**
1. Customer prepay
2. RBF
3. Grants
4. Scout checks and angels (no board)
5. Avoid: generalist seed VCs taking board seats

**Optionality (maximize fundraising path):**
1. Priced seed round with tier-1 or sector-specialist lead
2. Accelerator (tier 1)
3. Large angel round with name-brand angels

**Capital intensity (need meaningful dollars):**
1. Seed VC (sector or generalist)
2. Accelerator + follow-on angels
3. SBIR Phase II (if deep-tech fit)

**Founder is a repeat operator:**
- Angel SAFE with prior investors returning often closes fastest
- Tier-1 seed funds will engage pre-deck

**Founder is first-time, technical:**
- Accelerator is often the highest-leverage path; concentrate effort there

**Founder is first-time, non-technical:**
- Technical cofounder search usually precedes fundraising. Most seed funds will not back solo non-technical founders pre-product.

## 7. Red flags by instrument type

**SAFE / angel rounds:**
- Any term beyond post-money SAFE with cap and optional discount (no warrants, no most-favored-nation gymnastics, no side letters that alter economics)
- Accelerator-esque terms from an individual (10%+ for $50K is predatory)

**Syndicates:**
- Lead with short track record pushing large carry
- SPV terms that confuse dilution

**Accelerators:**
- Non-tier-1 taking >7% for under $200K
- "Accelerators" that are actually agencies extracting equity for services
- Mandatory relocation when program doesn't warrant it

**Rolling funds / micro-VC:**
- GP with no prior checks (rolling fund newcomers)
- Pro rata rights that are unusual in scope
- Unusual information rights

**Corporate / strategic:**
- Right of first refusal on future rounds
- Exclusivity clauses
- Observer seat with veto-like language

**RBF:**
- Personal guarantees
- Aggressive covenant triggers
- Revenue definitions that sweep in non-recurring

**Grants:**
- Matching funds you can't produce
- Sub-contracting requirements that consume the grant

**Customer prepay:**
- Service-level commitments you can't meet
- Refund clauses that make the cash effectively a loan

**Crowdfunding:**
- Platforms with opaque fee structures
- Campaigns priced at valuations the next round won't validate
