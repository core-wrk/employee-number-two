# Social Proof Framing

A working reference for `pitch-deck-builder`. How to present traction, customers, advisors, and early signal honestly.

The rule: present what you have at the highest truthful level, and do not reach for a level you haven't earned. Overclaiming is the fastest way to lose an investor during diligence; underclaiming wastes real progress.

---

## 1. Traction evidence hierarchy

From strongest to weakest:

1. **Paying customers with revenue** — monthly recurring revenue (MRR), retention visible
2. **Signed contracts** — counter-signed, legally binding, revenue starting on a future date
3. **Letters of intent (LOIs)** — non-binding but specific (named buyer, named price, named start date)
4. **Paid pilots** — customer paying for a time-boxed evaluation
5. **Free / design-partner pilots** — customer using the product under a structured partnership
6. **Strong verbal commits** — specific buyers saying "yes, when X ships I will buy" (record names and dates)
7. **Waitlist / interest signals** — email sign-ups, beta invites
8. **Customer interviews** — qualitative pain validation, no commitment

**Rule:** present at the highest rung you genuinely occupy. Skipping a rung (e.g., presenting waitlist as "demand") or conflating rungs (e.g., combining verbal commits and signed contracts into a "pipeline") is a diligence-killer.

---

## 2. Honest framing patterns for small-number traction

Three pilots beat a vague "$2M pipeline." Specificity builds trust.

**Template for signed contracts:**

> "[N] signed contracts totaling [$X] ARR, starting [date]. Named buyers: [Company A, Company B, Company C]. Average contract: [$Y]."

**Template for LOIs:**

> "[N] non-binding letters of intent from [named buyers] covering [specific use case] at anticipated [$X] ARR. Typical conversion from our LOI to contract in this segment: [rate] based on [source / analog]."

**Template for paid pilots:**

> "[N] paid pilots in progress. [X] of [N] have explicit conversion commitments at contract level. Current pilot ARR: [$Y]; anticipated ARR at conversion: [$Z]."

**Template for design partners:**

> "[N] design partners actively using the product weekly. Engagement: [N] of [N] use it at least 3x/week. [N] have verbally committed to paid conversion when [feature / milestone] ships."

**Template for verbal commits:**

> "[N] named buyers have committed verbally. Not yet papered. We are targeting [date] for first signed contract."

**Template for waitlist:**

> "[N] signups on the waitlist from [target ICP] contexts. [N] have completed a qualification call. Of those, [rate]% indicate intent to purchase."

Waitlist is weak signal. Don't inflate.

## 3. Quoting customers

**When direct quote helps:**
- Short, specific, reveals pain or transformation
- Attributable (full name + role + company)
- Reproduces verbatim how the customer actually speaks

**When paraphrase is safer:**
- Customer allows usage but not attribution
- Quote needs cleanup (but preserve meaning; do not invent phrases the customer didn't say)

**Rules:**
- Full attribution (name + title + company) unless explicitly asked to anonymize
- Get written permission before using in fundraising materials
- No composite quotes (stitching from multiple conversations)
- No invented enthusiasm ("revolutionary" is almost never how a real customer speaks)

**Anonymized quote format (when permission is limited):**

> "[Title] at a Fortune 500 financial services company" or "[Title] at a [stage] startup"

Do not say "a well-known [industry] company" — investors will press, and you will either break confidence or squirm.

## 4. Pipeline math

Pipelines are where honesty most often fails. The temptation is to sum every conversation.

**What pipeline is not:**
- Sum of LOI + verbal + cold conversations
- Expected value of every prospect at their "ideal" contract size
- Any number not backed by a specific named opportunity

**What pipeline is:**
- Named opportunities at a specific stage of your sales process
- Expected revenue if that opportunity converts
- Probability-weighted only if you have real historical conversion rates to weight by

**Defensible pipeline presentation:**

> "Qualified pipeline: [N] named opportunities. Stage breakdown:
> - Late stage (contract review): [N] opportunities, $[X] ARR potential
> - Mid stage (pilot or eval): [N] opportunities, $[Y] ARR potential
> - Early stage (discovery): [N] opportunities — not included in pipeline totals."

Do not sum to "$5M pipeline" if that implies expected revenue; only sum if explicitly labeling as "ARR at stake if all closed."

## 5. Advisor / investor name-drops

**When acceptable:**
- Advisor has signed a written advisor agreement and is actively engaged
- Investor has wired money (not "soft-circled" or "verbally committed")

**When not acceptable:**
- "Advising" based on a single conversation
- "Committed" based on a verbal interest with no docs
- Name-dropping someone's affiliation to imply backing they haven't given

**Rule:** if you would be embarrassed to ask the named person to confirm in front of the investor, don't use the name.

**Common mistake:** listing 6 advisors on the team slide to pad credibility. Investors discount heavily. Two truly active advisors > six decorative ones.

---

## 6. Anti-patterns (do not do these)

**"$2M pipeline" without methodology.** Collapses on first question.

**Logo soup on a traction slide without context.** Investors want to know the actual relationship (customer? pilot? design partner? evaluating?).

**Waitlist size as primary traction.** Waitlist without conversion context is a vanity metric.

**Charts with hidden y-axes.** If the MRR chart starts at $0 but the y-axis starts at $18K to exaggerate growth, that is dishonest framing.

**"We've been profiled in TechCrunch."** Coverage is not traction. Mention only if it generated measurable demand.

**Stock-photo testimonials.** Nobody is fooled.

**Inventing quotes.** Ever.

**"Revolutionary" / "game-changing" / "10x better."** Investors have heard these too many times; they signal hype over substance. If the product really is these things, the specifics should make the case without the adjectives.

**Hiding losses.** If you've lost customers or pilots, address it directly if asked. Do not pretend net retention is 100% when it's 80%.

**Vanity advisor logos.** Listing Stanford, MIT, or brand-name employers of advisors who do not substantively contribute.

## 7. What to say when traction is thin

If you are genuinely pre-traction, do not fake it. Reframe to insight + team.

**Pattern:**

- Traction slide becomes "Early Signal" slide
- Content: customer interviews (N+ conversations, specific patterns observed), design partners committed (named, with written agreement if possible), relevant founder / team context (prior exits, domain credibility, specific pattern-recognition)
- Emphasis shifts to: why this team + this insight justifies a pre-traction check

**Example honest framing:**

> "Early signal: 22 customer discovery conversations with [specific ICP]. Consistent pattern: [specific finding]. 3 named design partners have signed LOIs to pilot at $X when [v1 milestone]. Founder spent [N] years at [relevant company] in this exact workflow."

Investors who write pre-traction checks expect honesty about the stage. Pretending otherwise disqualifies you.

---

## 8. Checklist before finalizing the traction slide

- Every claim cites a specific evidence type from the hierarchy
- No rung-skipping (waitlist presented as revenue, verbals presented as signed)
- Every logo has a named relationship (customer / pilot / partner / evaluating)
- Every quote has attribution + permission (or is clearly anonymized)
- Pipeline math traces to named opportunities
- Y-axes on charts start at zero or are clearly labeled otherwise
- Advisor / investor claims are verifiable
- No composite or invented quotes
- Adjective density is low (the facts carry the case)
- If traction is thin, the slide is honestly "Early Signal" not fake traction
