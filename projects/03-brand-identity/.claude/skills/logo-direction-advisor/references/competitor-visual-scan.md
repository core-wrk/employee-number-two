# Competitor Visual Scan Framework

This is an internal reference for the `logo-direction-advisor` skill. It provides a structured framework for discussing the visual landscape of a founder's competitive space and identifying differentiation openings. **Do not show this document to the founder.** Use it to guide the conversation when the topic turns to competitive differentiation.

---

## How To Use This Guide

When the founder mentions competitors, you are not asking them to do an exhaustive brand audit. You are helping them notice patterns quickly, identify the dominant look, and find a distinctive angle. A good competitive scan takes 10-15 minutes of conversation, not an hour.

Walk the founder through the five dimensions below. Take notes. Use the **Pattern-Finding Questions** to surface what dominates. Use the **Differentiation Openings** to help them position.

---

## The Five Dimensions

For each competitor the founder names, listen for these five dimensions. You do not need to ask about each explicitly — let the founder describe what they see and tag the answers internally.

### 1. Mark type
- Wordmark / lettermark / symbol + wordmark / abstract mark / emblem
- Is there a dominant type across the space?

### 2. Letterform style (if wordmark or combination)
- Serif / sans / mono / custom
- Case: uppercase / lowercase / mixed
- Weight: light / regular / bold / heavy
- Character: geometric / humanist / neo-grotesque / transitional

### 3. Color character
- Monochrome (single-color logo) or multi-color
- Warm vs. cool
- Saturated vs. muted
- Is there a dominant color family in the space?

### 4. Shape vocabulary (if symbol or abstract)
- Geometric primitives (circle, square, triangle)
- Organic curves
- Literal representation (a thing) vs. abstract gesture
- Negative space plays

### 5. Overall temperature
- Corporate / playful / technical / luxury / utilitarian
- What does the category *feel* like as a whole?

---

## Pattern-Finding Questions

Ask these as the founder describes competitors. Do not ask all of them — ask the ones that feel relevant.

- "If I put all five of these logos in a row, what would they have in common?"
- "Is there a convention in the space — everyone using the same kind of mark or the same color family?"
- "Who in the space looks the *most* like everyone else? Who looks the *least*?"
- "Is there a logo in the space you wish you'd made? What specifically about it works?"
- "Is there a logo in the space you think is weak? What's weak about it?"

---

## Dominant Patterns by Space (Common Examples)

Use these as conversation primers if you recognize the space, but never recite them as facts. They are starting points, not truths.

- **B2B SaaS** — Lowercase geometric sans wordmark in a single brand color. High saturation for consumer-facing, muted for enterprise. Example pattern: Stripe, Linear, Notion, Vercel.
- **Fintech / banking** — Deep blue, serif or transitional sans, emphasis on trust signals. Newer fintechs break with bright primaries.
- **Consumer DTC** — Custom wordmarks with personality, often serif or display type, warmer palettes. Example pattern: Warby Parker, Allbirds, Glossier.
- **Developer tools** — Monospace-inspired, abstract marks, dark mode first. Example pattern: GitHub, Railway, Supabase.
- **Wellness / health** — Sans serif, soft colors, minimal marks, rounded edges. Convention-heavy space.
- **AI / ML** — Geometric abstract marks, gradients, often a glyph + wordmark. Fast-moving conventions.
- **Climate / sustainability** — Green dominant, organic shapes, often illustrative. Differentiation by breaking from the green-leaf trope is increasingly common.

---

## Differentiation Openings

Once the dominant pattern is identified, help the founder see 2-3 specific ways to stand apart. Use these openings:

### 1. Invert the dominant mark type
If everyone in the space uses wordmarks, a well-designed symbol stands out. If everyone uses symbols, a strong custom wordmark stands out. Warn the founder that inversion has to be earned — the atypical choice needs to be distinctly good, not just different.

### 2. Invert the dominant color
If the space is blue-dominant, a warm color (coral, amber, terracotta) stands out without feeling random. If the space is pastel, a confident deep color stands out.

### 3. Invert the dominant character
If everyone in the space looks serious and corporate, a playful or warm mark stands out. If everyone is playful, a restrained and confident mark stands out.

### 4. Borrow from an adjacent category
If the space has visual conventions the founder wants to break, borrow from a category with different conventions. A wellness brand that borrows developer-tool minimalism, or a B2B SaaS that borrows DTC craft, can look distinctively confident.

### 5. Lean hard into a specific craft cue
Custom letterforms, an unusual ligature, a mark that reveals meaning only on second look, or a shape that plays with negative space. These require designer skill but create memorability the space lacks.

## When To Push Back

- **Founder wants to look "exactly like" an admired competitor** — "That logo works because of what they already are. If yours looks identical, you'll be mistaken for them. What makes you different that should show up visually?"
- **Founder thinks the space has no conventions** — "Every space has conventions, even if they're loose. Let's spend two minutes finding them so we can choose whether to follow or break them."
- **Founder wants to ignore competitors entirely** — "I'm not suggesting you copy them. But you have to know what the room looks like before you decide what to wear."
- **Founder fixates on a single competitor they admire** — "One reference is a fixation. Three references is a frame. Let's find two more."

## Output to the Brief

The conclusions from this scan feed directly into two sections of `outputs/logo-concepts.md`:

- **Competitive Differentiation** — The specific differentiation angle(s) you chose and why
- **Must-Avoids** — The dominant patterns or clichés you decided to reject

Make sure both sections are populated before writing the brief.
