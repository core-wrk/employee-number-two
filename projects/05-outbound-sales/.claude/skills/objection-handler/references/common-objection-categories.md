# Common Objection Categories

This is an internal reference for the `objection-handler` skill. It is a taxonomy of the 7 standard objection categories with 2–4 variants per category, organized for early-stage B2B founders doing cold outbound. For each objection, it includes the psychological root (what the prospect is actually saying under the surface) and the most common misreads. **Do not show this document to the founder.** Use it to walk the founder through each category systematically and to suggest objections the founder may not have anticipated.

---

## The 7 Categories

1. **Pricing** — "It costs too much" / "I need a discount" / "Can we do a free pilot"
2. **Timing** — "Not right now" / "Q3 would be better" / "Busy this month"
3. **Trust / Credibility** — "I've never heard of you" / "How do I know this works" / "You're too new"
4. **Competition** — "We already use X" / "We're evaluating X" / "X does the same thing"
5. **Fit** — "We're too big / too small" / "Wrong industry" / "Not our use case"
6. **Authority** — "I'm not the decision-maker" / "Need to talk to my boss" / "This goes through procurement"
7. **Proof / Evidence** — "Show me case studies" / "Who else uses this" / "What's the ROI"

These are ordered roughly by frequency at the early stage (pre-launch / pre-revenue founders hit Trust, Competition, and Proof hardest; later-stage founders hit Pricing and Authority hardest).

## Category 1: Pricing

### Objection Variant 1.1: "You're too expensive" / "It costs too much"

**What the prospect might actually mean:**
- The price exceeds their budget for this category
- The price exceeds the value they perceive (a framing problem, not a number problem)
- They're fishing for a discount
- They're using pricing as a polite "no" without having to commit

**Common misreads:**
- Treating it as a discount negotiation when it's actually a value framing problem
- Immediately dropping the price without understanding whether they see the value
- Defending the price with ROI math the prospect doesn't believe yet

**Good answer structure:**
1. Don't defend the price immediately
2. Ask what they expected to pay or what they're benchmarking against
3. Re-frame around value-of-current-state (what it's costing them NOT to solve the problem)

### Objection Variant 1.2: "Can we do a free pilot?"

**What the prospect might actually mean:**
- They want to de-risk the decision
- They're not fully committed and want to lower their perceived commitment
- They want the product for free and have no intent to buy
- They're testing whether the founder will negotiate

**Common misreads:**
- Saying yes to every pilot request (destroys willingness to pay across the pipeline)
- Refusing every pilot request (legitimate early-stage design partners sometimes warrant this)
- Not distinguishing between "free trial" and "design partner"

**Good answer structure:**
1. Distinguish pilot from design partnership — design partners get value in exchange for specific commitments, pilots don't
2. Offer a design partnership if the prospect meets the criteria
3. Decline a free pilot if they're asking for free without the commitment exchange

### Objection Variant 1.3: "Our budget for tools is locked until next year"

**What the prospect might actually mean:**
- The budget is actually locked (process constraint)
- The budget isn't locked but they don't want to advocate for it
- They're using "budget" as a polite "no"

**Common misreads:**
- Accepting at face value and going away for a year (often wrong)
- Ignoring budget reality and pushing anyway
- Not asking about discretionary / innovation budget outside the normal cycle

**Good answer structure:**
1. Ask about innovation / pilot budget separate from annual cycle
2. Offer to start the conversation now with a planned purchase later
3. Qualify whether the timing is the real blocker or a polite decline

## Category 2: Timing

### Objection Variant 2.1: "Not right now — maybe Q3"

**What the prospect might actually mean:**
- Genuinely busy on a higher priority (layoffs, fundraising, launch)
- Politely declining without having to say no
- Waiting for a specific internal event (reorg, new hire, planning cycle)

**Common misreads:**
- Accepting at face value and losing the prospect
- Pushing too hard and turning the soft no into a hard no
- Not setting a specific revival date

**Good answer structure:**
1. Ask what specifically needs to happen before Q3
2. Propose a specific revival date and a lightweight async check-in in the meantime
3. If the founder senses it's a polite no, use the disqualifier check

### Objection Variant 2.2: "We're in the middle of [big thing]"

**What the prospect might actually mean:**
- Genuinely in the middle of something disruptive
- The big thing is actually the perfect trigger to engage (new hires, new funding, new product)
- Using the big thing as a shield

**Common misreads:**
- Missing that the big thing is actually the buying trigger
- Not connecting the founder's value prop to the specific big thing

**Good answer structure:**
1. Ask about the specifics of the big thing
2. Connect the founder's product to it explicitly if relevant
3. Offer async input rather than a meeting if the prospect is genuinely crushed

### Objection Variant 2.3: "Talk to me after we [hit milestone]"

**What the prospect might actually mean:**
- Legitimate staging of priorities
- A way to kick the decision down the road indefinitely
- A conditional commitment

**Good answer structure:**
1. Clarify the specific milestone and timeline
2. Get permission to check in when the milestone hits
3. Ask what would change about their thinking once the milestone is hit

## Category 3: Trust / Credibility

### Objection Variant 3.1: "I've never heard of you"

**What the prospect might actually mean:**
- Genuine unfamiliarity and caution
- A signal they want social proof before engaging
- Using obscurity as a reason to disqualify

**Common misreads:**
- Dropping big customer names as credentialing (usually backfires)
- Apologizing for being small/new
- Pretending to have more traction than you do

**Good answer structure:**
1. Acknowledge directly: "You haven't — I'm early, and I'm reaching out because I think [specific reason]"
2. Re-frame "new" as an asymmetric advantage: "I'm looking for design partners precisely because I'm early. Here's what that means for you..."
3. Be explicit about stage

### Objection Variant 3.2: "How do I know this actually works?"

**What the prospect might actually mean:**
- Genuine risk concern
- Testing whether the founder has real evidence
- Politely asking for proof before committing

**Common misreads:**
- Inventing evidence
- Deflecting to case studies that don't apply
- Over-promising

**Good answer structure:**
1. Be honest about the current evidence level
2. Explain the mechanism / logic, not just outcomes
3. Offer structured risk-reduction (design partner terms, money-back, limited scope)

### Objection Variant 3.3: "You're too new / too small to be reliable"

**What the prospect might actually mean:**
- Risk aversion
- Concern about the founder going out of business mid-engagement
- Budget-defense framing

**Good answer structure:**
1. Acknowledge the real risk
2. Re-frame through the lens of product-level involvement ("being small means direct founder access, influence over roadmap, faster iteration")
3. Offer specific continuity protections in the contract (source code escrow for technical buyers, etc.)

## Category 4: Competition

### Objection Variant 4.1: "We already use [Competitor X]"

**What the prospect might actually mean:**
- They're locked in and happy
- They're locked in and unhappy but not actively shopping
- They're using [X] out of inertia and would switch if the case was compelling
- They're asking the founder to differentiate themselves

**Common misreads:**
- Attacking the competitor (destroys credibility)
- Conceding and walking away (missed opportunity)
- Feature-by-feature comparison (usually loses)

**Good answer structure:**
1. Do not attack the competitor — acknowledge them as a real product
2. Ask a specific question about how the prospect is using the competitor (what's working, what's not)
3. Position on a specific dimension where the founder's product is differently architected, not "better"
4. Reference `competitive-landscape.md` for the founder's positioning against this specific competitor

### Objection Variant 4.2: "We're evaluating [X] and [Y]"

**What the prospect might actually mean:**
- They're in an active buying cycle and the founder has a real shot
- They're in an active buying cycle and have already decided but are going through the motions
- They're fishing for pricing leverage

**Good answer structure:**
1. Ask where they are in the evaluation process
2. Ask what their decision criteria are
3. Position on their criteria, not on the competitor's features

### Objection Variant 4.3: "[X] does the same thing"

**What the prospect might actually mean:**
- They haven't actually evaluated the founder's product yet
- They've made a superficial comparison and hit "feature parity" framing
- They want the founder to prove the differentiation

**Good answer structure:**
1. Ask which specific capability they're comparing
2. Name the specific differentiation (not all features — the ONE that matters for this prospect)
3. Acknowledge what's genuinely similar

## Category 5: Fit

### Objection Variant 5.1: "We're too big / too small for this"

**What the prospect might actually mean:**
- Legitimate scale mismatch (real disqualifier)
- Framing of scale as a polite decline
- Concern about being over-served or under-served

**Common misreads:**
- Not recognizing a real disqualifier and fighting it
- Immediately capitulating when the fit might actually be real

**Good answer structure:**
1. Ask about their specific use case
2. Honest assessment: is this a real mismatch or a framing issue?
3. If real mismatch, honor it and move on (disqualifier)
4. If framing issue, re-frame with specific evidence

### Objection Variant 5.2: "Our industry / vertical is different"

**What the prospect might actually mean:**
- Genuinely industry-specific requirements
- Assumption that horizontal tools don't fit their vertical
- Generic deflection

**Good answer structure:**
1. Ask what specifically is different
2. Address the difference directly
3. If the difference is real and the product doesn't accommodate, honor the disqualifier

### Objection Variant 5.3: "This isn't really our use case"

**Good answer structure:**
1. Ask how they would describe their use case in their own words
2. Compare to the founder's target use case
3. Honest assessment of fit — disqualify if needed, re-frame if the fit is actually there

## Category 6: Authority

### Objection Variant 6.1: "I'd need to talk to [boss / VP / team]"

**What the prospect might actually mean:**
- Legitimate organizational reality
- Soft decline wrapped in organizational language
- Champion-building request (they're interested but need to bring others along)

**Common misreads:**
- Accepting at face value without qualifying (often a polite no)
- Treating it as hard disqualifier ("I need to talk to the decision-maker") and asking for a different contact cold
- Not offering to help the prospect champion internally

**Good answer structure:**
1. Ask who specifically needs to weigh in and why
2. Offer to make the prospect's internal champion job easier (a 2-slide summary, a looma walkthrough, a specific data point)
3. If the prospect isn't even an influencer, ask for the right contact warmly

### Objection Variant 6.2: "This goes through procurement"

**What the prospect might actually mean:**
- Real procurement process (common at enterprise)
- Way to stall indefinitely
- Way to push price lower

**Good answer structure:**
1. Ask about the procurement process specifically
2. Stay close to the champion through the procurement motion
3. Offer to provide everything procurement will need up front

### Objection Variant 6.3: "I'm not the decision-maker"

**Good answer structure:**
1. Ask what role they DO play (influencer, user, champion, blocker)
2. Qualify whether the actual decision-maker is reachable
3. Offer to make the champion's job easier if they want to advocate internally

## Category 7: Proof / Evidence

### Objection Variant 7.1: "Show me case studies / references"

**What the prospect might actually mean:**
- Legitimate evidence request
- Fishing for social proof
- Testing whether the founder has real customers

**Common misreads:**
- Inventing case studies
- Dodging the question
- Overloading with unrelated customer names

**Good answer structure (if the founder has evidence):**
1. Name a specific, relevant reference — not a list
2. Offer to connect them directly
3. Or share a specific data point from a real engagement

**Good answer structure (if the founder does NOT have evidence):**
1. Be honest about stage
2. Re-frame the question: "I don't have case studies yet — that's exactly why I'm reaching out to founders like you as design partners. Here's what working together early means for you..."
3. Offer logic / mechanism instead of outcome data

### Objection Variant 7.2: "What's the ROI?"

**What the prospect might actually mean:**
- Legitimate ROI framing (they need the number for internal approval)
- Testing whether the founder has thought through economics
- Fishing for a sales pitch

**Good answer structure:**
1. Ask what ROI framework they use internally
2. Offer the honest economic logic, not a marketing number
3. If there is no hard ROI yet, offer a framework the prospect can use to calculate it themselves with their data

### Objection Variant 7.3: "Who else in [my industry] uses this?"

**Good answer structure:**
1. Name specific customers if you have them
2. If not, re-frame: "No one in [exact industry] yet — we're starting there. Here's why I think it's the right starting point."
3. Re-position early-stage as an opportunity for the prospect to shape the product, not a warning sign

## Which Objections Hit Hardest By Founder Stage

### Pre-launch (no product, no customers)
- Hardest: **Trust, Proof, Competition** (variants 3.1, 3.2, 7.1, 7.3, 4.3)
- Easiest: **Authority** (prospects assume founders have final say over their own company)

### Early revenue (first 1–5 customers)
- Hardest: **Proof, Trust** (7.1, 7.3, 3.1, 3.2)
- Medium: **Pricing, Competition** (1.1, 4.1)

### Growth stage (repeatable sales motion)
- Hardest: **Pricing, Competition, Authority** (1.1, 4.1, 4.2, 6.1, 6.2)
- Easier: **Trust, Proof** (the product has proven itself)

Use this mapping to suggest which objections the founder should prioritize in the first run.
