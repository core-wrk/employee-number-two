# Objection Response Patterns

This is an internal reference for the `objection-handler` skill. It is the set of response patterns used to structure answers across the 7 categories, with worked examples. **Do not show this document to the founder.** Use it to scaffold the founder's answers without dictating the exact words — the words need to come from the founder in their own voice.

---

## The Four Patterns

Objection responses at this stage follow one of four core patterns:

1. **Acknowledge, Reframe, Redirect (ARR)** — for most objections where the prospect has a legitimate concern
2. **Yes-And** — for objections that are partially true, where the founder wants to validate the point before offering a different framing
3. **Feel / Felt / Found** — for social proof moments (use sparingly — easy to overuse and sound scripted)
4. **Validate, Clarify, Reframe (VCR)** — for objections that are ambiguous and might be polite declines

**Plus one anti-pattern that every answer needs:**

5. **The Disqualifier Check** — recognizing when an objection is actually a disqualification and stopping the conversation gracefully

---

## Pattern 1: Acknowledge, Reframe, Redirect (ARR)

**When to use:** The default pattern. Works for most objections where the prospect is raising a real concern and wants a real response.

### Structure

1. **Acknowledge** — validate the concern briefly. Do not dismiss. Do not over-agree.
2. **Reframe** — offer a different way of thinking about the concern that changes what matters.
3. **Redirect** — move the conversation forward with a question or a specific next step.

### Worked example — "You're too expensive"

> **Acknowledge:** "I hear you — pricing is always the right question at this stage."
>
> **Reframe:** "Can I ask what you're benchmarking against? Because the way I think about it, the real cost isn't the price of the tool — it's the cost of the current state, which is [specific thing]. If a new SDR is ramping 45 days slower than they could be, that's [X] in quota dollars, and that's the number I'd compare against."
>
> **Redirect:** "Would it make sense to talk through how you'd measure that at NorthStar specifically?"

### Worked example — "I've never heard of you"

> **Acknowledge:** "You haven't — I'm early."
>
> **Reframe:** "That's actually why I reached out to you specifically. I'm looking for design partners at exactly your stage and role, and 'early' means you'd have direct influence over product direction in a way you won't with a more mature tool."
>
> **Redirect:** "Does that kind of working relationship appeal to you, or are you looking for something more off-the-shelf?"

### Why ARR works

It respects the prospect's intelligence. It doesn't dismiss the concern, doesn't over-apologize, and doesn't pretend the concern is wrong. It moves the conversation forward without pretending the founder has all the answers.

### Failure modes

- **Skipping the acknowledgment** — jumping straight to the reframe sounds defensive
- **Over-acknowledging** — "You're so right, pricing is so hard!" sounds weak
- **Reframing without a new frame** — if the reframe is just "but actually it's worth it," that's not a reframe
- **Redirecting without moving forward** — "good question, let's move on" is not a redirect

## Pattern 2: Yes-And

**When to use:** When the objection contains a genuinely correct observation that the founder wants to validate before offering a different angle. Works especially well for competition objections.

### Structure

1. **Yes** — agree with the specific true thing in the objection
2. **And** — add something that doesn't contradict but shifts the frame

### Worked example — "[Competitor X] already does this"

> **Yes:** "You're right — [Competitor X] does have this capability, and they're a real product. I wouldn't tell you otherwise."
>
> **And:** "The way I'm thinking about it differently is [specific architectural or positioning difference]. For you specifically, I think the relevant question is [specific question about their use case]. Would it be useful to talk through how each would work for what you're actually trying to do?"

### Worked example — "I'd need to talk to my VP Sales first"

> **Yes:** "Makes sense — enablement tool decisions usually go through sales at companies your size."
>
> **And:** "What would make that conversation easier for you? I can put together a 2-slide summary or record a 3-minute loom walkthrough that you can forward if it helps. I've done that for a few design partners and the VP usually responds faster when the context is already framed."

### Why Yes-And works

It's disarming. The prospect expects the founder to argue; when the founder agrees with the true part of the objection, it signals confidence and earns trust. The "and" then opens space for a new angle without it feeling like a pivot.

### Failure modes

- **Yes-but instead of Yes-and** — "Yes, but..." undoes the acknowledgment and sounds defensive
- **Yessing the wrong thing** — if the founder yes-ands a factually wrong claim, they lose credibility
- **Missing the "and"** — just agreeing without adding anything concedes the objection

## Pattern 3: Feel / Felt / Found

**When to use:** For social proof moments when the founder can cite a real customer or reference. **Use sparingly** — the pattern is well-known and can sound scripted if overused.

### Structure

1. **Feel** — "I understand why you feel that way..."
2. **Felt** — "...other customers have felt the same thing..."
3. **Found** — "...and what they found is [specific outcome]"

### Worked example — "I'm worried this won't work for a team my size"

> **Feel:** "That's a legitimate concern — scale matters in this category."
>
> **Felt:** "A design partner at [similar-size company] had the same worry coming in."
>
> **Found:** "What they found was that the tool scales by configuration rather than by headcount, so the [specific mechanism] worked exactly the same for their 15-person team as it does for 150. I can connect you to them directly if it'd help."

### Why Feel/Felt/Found works

It validates the emotion, uses social proof, and offers specific evidence. It's efficient.

### Failure modes

- **Fake customers** — if the founder doesn't actually have a [similar-size company] to reference, this pattern is a lie. Never fake.
- **Overuse** — the pattern is recognizable. Founders who use it on every objection sound like they're running through a checklist.
- **Vague "found" statement** — "they found it worked great" is weak. The "found" needs to be specific.
- **Using without a real reference to offer** — if the founder can't actually connect the prospect to the cited customer, the pattern collapses

## Pattern 4: Validate, Clarify, Reframe (VCR)

**When to use:** For ambiguous objections that might be polite declines. The pattern is designed to tell the difference before committing to a long response.

### Structure

1. **Validate** — briefly acknowledge the concern
2. **Clarify** — ask a specific question to understand what they actually mean
3. **Reframe** (only if warranted by the clarification) — if the concern is real, reframe; if it's a polite decline, gracefully exit

### Worked example — "Maybe in Q3"

> **Validate:** "Q3 makes sense — planning cycles matter."
>
> **Clarify:** "Is there something specific about Q3 that would change the picture — a new budget, a team transition, a project wrapping — or is this more of a 'not urgent right now' thing?"
>
> **Reframe (if the clarify reveals a real trigger):** "Got it. Would it make sense to check back in [specific timing tied to the trigger]? I'll put a note on my calendar."
>
> **Graceful exit (if the clarify reveals a polite decline):** "Totally hear you. If it doesn't become urgent, no hard feelings — I'd rather hear 'not now' clearly than spend time we both don't have. Is there a better signal I should be watching for?"

### Why VCR works

It gives the founder real information about whether to pursue or step back. Saves time for both sides when the prospect isn't actually interested.

### Failure modes

- **Skipping the clarify** — jumping to reframe without clarifying commits the founder to a pursuit that might be wasted
- **Asking a yes/no clarify question** — "is this a no?" forces the prospect into awkwardness. Open-ended clarify questions work better.
- **Not exiting gracefully** — if the clarify reveals a polite decline, forcing the reframe anyway annoys the prospect and burns the relationship

## Pattern 5: The Disqualifier Check

**When to use:** On every objection. This is not a response pattern; it is a check the founder applies to every objection to distinguish "legitimate concern worth responding to" from "polite decline the founder should honor and move on from."

### The check

For every objection, the founder should ask internally: **"Is this an objection I can answer, or is this actually a 'no' in different clothes?"**

Signals that suggest a polite decline (disqualifier, not objection):

- The prospect gives multiple objections in sequence — each gets answered, next one comes up, and the conversation never advances
- The prospect uses definitive language ("we don't need this at all", "this isn't a priority at all")
- The prospect is unwilling to commit to any next step — not a meeting, not an async review, not a check-in
- The prospect's objection is structurally about fit (size, industry, use case), not about friction
- The prospect explicitly disqualifies themselves ("you're not in our category", "we don't buy tools in this space")

Signals that suggest a real objection (worth answering):

- The prospect offers one specific concern and asks a real question about it
- The prospect proposes a conditional ("if X were true, I'd be interested")
- The prospect asks about a next step even while raising concerns
- The prospect's tone is engaged, not dismissive
- The prospect brings it up proactively rather than using it to end the call

### What to do when you spot a disqualifier

Honor it. Move on gracefully. Do not try to convert a disqualifier into an objection — it wastes the founder's time, annoys the prospect, and burns future goodwill.

**The graceful exit:**

> "Sounds like this isn't the right fit right now, and I respect that. If your situation changes — [specific trigger] — I'm an email away. Otherwise, wishing you well with [specific thing about their company]."

Mark the prospect as Lost in `pipeline-tracker.md`, capture the reason in the Notes field (this is valuable data for ICP refinement), and move on.

## When To Combine Patterns

- **ARR + Yes-And** — works well for competition objections: yes-and the competitor's legitimacy, then ARR to differentiate
- **VCR + ARR** — for ambiguous timing objections: clarify first, then reframe if warranted
- **Feel/Felt/Found + ARR** — for trust objections where the founder has a reference: acknowledge the concern (ARR), then offer the reference (FFF)

Do NOT combine patterns into a scripted routine. The patterns are scaffolding, not scripts. The founder's actual response should sound natural, draw from the pattern mentally, and use the founder's own voice — not recite a framework.

---

## The Meta-Rule: The Founder Is Not A Salesperson

At this stage, the founder is not in sales mode — they are in learning mode. Every objection is a learning opportunity, not a battle to win. The goal of an objection response is not to "overcome" the objection and close the deal. The goal is to:

1. Learn whether the objection is real (and update the playbook with it)
2. Learn whether the prospect is a real buyer (and update the pipeline tracker accordingly)
3. Leave the prospect thinking "this founder is thoughtful and honest" regardless of outcome

Founders who treat every objection as a battle burn out their own pipeline. Founders who treat objections as information-gathering opportunities build relationships that convert later, even if the immediate call doesn't close.

When in doubt, ask a clarifying question instead of answering. The clarification often reveals the real concern underneath, and the real concern is usually different from the surface objection.
