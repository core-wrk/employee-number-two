# Outreach Sequence Template

This is an internal reference for the `email-drafter` skill. It is the 4-touch sequence structure for cold outbound, with rhetorical job, character budget, opening pattern, and failure modes for each touch. **Do not show this document to the founder.** Use it to calibrate every draft to its position in the sequence.

---

## The 4-Touch Sequence

The default outbound sequence is 4 touches over ~3 weeks:

| Touch | Day | Purpose | Word target | Failure mode |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Initial | 0 | Introduce and propose a low-friction ask | ~100–150 words (email) / ~300 chars (DM opener) | Feature-listing, wall of text, vague ask |
| Follow-up 1 | 3–5 | New angle, re-state ask | ~75–100 words | "Just following up" opener |
| Follow-up 2 | 10 | Acknowledge silence, final substantive ask | ~50–75 words | Guilt trip, fake urgency |
| Breakup | 21 | Graceful final touch, door open | ~50 words | Passive-aggressive, hard ask |

After the breakup, if there is no reply, the prospect moves to Stalled in `pipeline-tracker.md`. Do not send a 5th touch — it crosses the line from persistent to annoying and damages sender reputation.

---

## Touch 1: Initial

### Rhetorical job

Earn 20 seconds of the prospect's attention. That is the entire job. You are not trying to close a deal, book a meeting, or deliver a pitch. You are trying to get the prospect to read the email all the way through and think "this is actually about me" — not "I've seen 200 of these this month."

### Structure

4 components, each 1–2 sentences:

1. **Opener (personalization)** — the specific thing about this prospect that proves the email was written by a human for them, not a template filled in with their name. This is the make-or-break line. If the opener is generic, the rest does not matter.
2. **Bridge (pain/relevance)** — connect the opener to the problem the founder is solving. "This usually breaks down when..." or "The pattern I see in companies like yours is..."
3. **Value line (one claim)** — one sentence, grounded in the product or the founder's insight, tied to the pillar from `messaging-framework.md`. Not a feature list. One claim.
4. **Ask (specific, low-friction)** — "15 minutes next Tuesday or Thursday" is good. "Would love to chat about how we can help" is bad. Name a specific next step. If the ask is a meeting, give two time windows.

### Word target

**100–150 words for cold email.** Under 100 is hard to fit all 4 components; over 150 gets scrolled past.

**~300 characters for LinkedIn DM opener.** LinkedIn DMs collapse if they're too long; the prospect sees only the first few lines in their inbox. Keep it tight.

### Subject line (for email)

The subject line is its own craft. The goal is to earn the open, not to summarize the email. Patterns that work:

- **Specific reference:** "Your SDR hires at NorthStar" — references the personalization angle directly
- **Contrarian question:** "Is ramp time actually a people problem?" — names the problem and disagrees with the conventional framing
- **Short and concrete:** "Quick question about [specific thing]" — only works if the "quick question" is actually quick
- **Mutual connection:** "[Connection name] mentioned you" — only if true, and only with permission from the connection

Patterns that do NOT work:
- "Following up" / "Quick check-in" / "Touching base" — all generic openers, all scrolled past
- "Partnership opportunity" / "Exploring synergies" — translates to "I want to sell you something"
- Anything with an emoji at the start
- Anything with ALL CAPS
- Anything longer than 7 words

### Failure modes to refuse

1. **Feature-listing** — 5 bullets of product capabilities. The initial touch is about the prospect, not the product.
2. **Wall of text** — 300+ words. The prospect reads the first two lines and closes.
3. **Vague ask** — "Would love to connect" / "Curious to hear your thoughts." Say what you actually want.
4. **"I hope this email finds you well"** — flagged, auto-ignored.
5. **Self-congratulatory intro** — "We're the leading platform for [category]..." The prospect doesn't care.
6. **Name-dropping irrelevant customers** — "We work with Acme Corp and BigCo." Unless the name-drop is specifically relevant to the prospect's context, it reads as credentialing theater.

## Touch 2: Follow-up 1

### Rhetorical job

Assume the initial touch was missed, not ignored. Most cold emails are missed — the inbox is noisy, the prospect is busy, timing is random. Follow-up 1 is a polite "in case you missed it" that brings a new angle so it's not just a repeat.

### Structure

3 components:

1. **Light acknowledgment (one line)** — "Following up on my note from last week — wanted to share one more thing." Do NOT use "Just following up" or "Checking in" as the opening line of either the subject or the body.
2. **New angle** — this is the critical piece. Bring something new to the conversation: a new piece of research, a new case study, a new observation, a link to a relevant article, a new framing of the problem. The new angle proves the founder has been thinking about the prospect, not just setting reminders.
3. **Lighter-friction alternative ask** — if the initial touch asked for a 15-minute meeting, the follow-up can offer an easier alternative: "If a call is too much right now, I'd love to send you a 2-minute loom walking through what I'm thinking. Any interest?"

### Word target

**75–100 words.** Shorter than the initial because you are building on context, not establishing it.

### Subject line (for email)

Do NOT use "RE: [original subject line]" unless you are genuinely replying to an earlier email thread. Do NOT use "Following up on my previous email." Use a fresh subject line that references the new angle:

- "One more thing about your SDR hires"
- "Thought you'd find this useful"
- "A different angle on the enablement question"

### Failure modes to refuse

1. **"Just following up"** — scrolled past instantly.
2. **Guilt trip** — "Did you see my last email?" Reads as entitled.
3. **Same message repeated** — no new angle. The prospect's inbox treats this as spam.
4. **More aggressive ask** — escalating from "15 minutes" to "30 minutes" is backwards. Follow-ups should lower friction, not raise it.

## Touch 3: Follow-up 2

### Rhetorical job

Last substantive touch. You are acknowledging the silence directly, re-stating why this is worth the prospect's attention, and making a final ask before graceful exit. The tone shifts slightly — more direct, less salesy, more "I think this is worth your 10 minutes and here's my last try."

### Structure

3 components, shorter than Follow-up 1:

1. **Acknowledge the silence directly** — "I know you're slammed — I'll make this quick" or "I've sent two notes already and I promise this is the last substantive one." Direct acknowledgment is disarming; evasion is not.
2. **Compressed value re-statement** — one sentence. Strip everything. Just the core reason this matters for this specific prospect.
3. **Final substantive ask** — specific, low-friction, with an explicit door-close: "If [specific low-friction ask], great. If not, I'll send one more short note and then stop."

### Word target

**50–75 words.** Tight.

### Subject line (for email)

- "Last substantive note — [topic]"
- "One more thing, then I'll stop"
- "Final pitch on [topic]"

### Failure modes to refuse

1. **Fake urgency** — "This offer expires Friday." There is no offer.
2. **Emotional manipulation** — "I really need your feedback" / "I'd hate for you to miss this." The prospect owes you nothing.
3. **Bigger ask** — follow-ups should continue lowering friction, not raising it.
4. **Pretending to not know this is the 3rd touch** — acknowledge the count, don't hide it.

## Touch 4: Breakup

### Rhetorical job

Graceful exit. Explicit "I'll stop reaching out after this" with the door left open for the future. Breakups are the most-responded-to touch in many sequences — the social dynamic of "I'm about to stop" triggers the prospect to respond if they had any interest at all. This is a feature of how humans work, not a manipulation.

### Structure

3 components:

1. **Explicit breakup** — "I've reached out a few times without hearing back, so I'll take this as a 'not right now' and stop." One sentence, direct, no guilt.
2. **Parting value or useful close** — a short thought, a useful link, a well-wish. This is what makes the breakup graceful instead of petulant.
3. **Door left open** — "If your situation changes and this becomes relevant, I'm an email away. Otherwise, wishing you luck with [specific thing about their company]."

### Word target

**~50 words.** Short and clean.

### Subject line (for email)

- "Closing the loop"
- "One last note"
- "Stopping my outreach — but wanted to leave this"

### Failure modes to refuse

1. **Passive-aggressive** — "I guess you don't need [our solution]" / "I'll stop bothering you." Reads as petty.
2. **Guilt** — "I've tried reaching out multiple times..." Just stop. Don't make the prospect feel bad.
3. **Final ask** — a breakup is not an ask. The door left open is implicit, not an action.
4. **Overlong** — if the breakup is 200 words, it is not a breakup. It is a 5th pitch.
5. **Sarcastic** — dry humor does not land in a cold email context. The prospect has no context for the founder's voice.

## Sequence Timing Rules

| Touch | Day | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Initial | 0 | — |
| Follow-up 1 | Day 3–5 | Inside one business week; the prospect may still remember the initial touch |
| Follow-up 2 | Day 10 | Second business week; natural rhythm for a "second follow-up" without feeling rushed |
| Breakup | Day 21 | Third business week; enough time to confirm silence is silence, not delay |

**Do not compress the sequence to less than 14 days.** Cramming 4 touches into 5 days reads as desperate.

**Do not stretch the sequence to more than 28 days.** After 4 weeks, the prospect has no memory of the initial touch; you have to start over as a fresh cold email, which means the sequence has effectively died.

## LinkedIn DM Sequence Modifications

LinkedIn DMs work the same way, but with these modifications:

- **Initial touch word target drops to ~300 characters for the first message**, then a second message 1–2 days later can be longer if the first one lands (a reply or a read receipt)
- **Follow-up 1 can be a connection request with a note** if the initial DM did not land
- **Follow-up 2 can be an engagement-based touch** (commenting on their post, reacting to their content) rather than a direct message — this is softer and often more effective
- **Breakup on LinkedIn is optional** — it's more natural to just stop DMing than to send an explicit breakup. Use founder's judgment.

## When To Abandon The Sequence

Some signals mean "stop sending follow-ups" even if the sequence is not complete:

- The prospect explicitly declines → move to Lost, stop sending
- The prospect says "not right now, try me in Q3" → move to Stalled with a revival date, stop sending
- The prospect replies to Follow-up 1 or 2 → move to Replied, switch out of the sequence and into a conversation
- The prospect's email bounces → move to Lost with a note, stop sending (and flag for contact re-enrichment)
- The prospect's auto-responder says they left the company → move to Lost, stop sending

The sequence is the default path for "silence." Any signal that is not silence exits the sequence immediately.
