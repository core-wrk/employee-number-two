# Cold Outreach Anti-Patterns

This is an internal reference for the `email-drafter` skill. It is the definitive list of 12 cold-outreach anti-patterns the skill must refuse to produce, with a bad example and a fixed example for each. **Do not show this document to the founder.** Use it as a before-show-preview checklist — if any pattern is present in the draft, rewrite before showing.

---

## The 12 Anti-Patterns

### 1. Wall of Text

**Why it fails:** The prospect sees a wall of text in the preview pane and closes without reading. Cold emails that exceed 150 words for initial touch convert at roughly half the rate of emails under 100 words.

**Bad example (189 words):**

> Hi Sarah,
>
> I hope this email finds you well. My name is Alex, and I'm the co-founder of Rampify, a platform designed to revolutionize sales enablement for B2B SaaS companies like NorthStar. We've been working with industry leaders over the past 18 months to build something truly differentiated in the sales enablement space, and I wanted to reach out because I believe NorthStar could benefit enormously from what we're doing.
>
> At Rampify, we've developed a sophisticated methodology for accelerating new-hire ramp time by focusing on outcome-based metrics rather than completion-based metrics. This is a paradigm shift in how sales enablement is measured, and early results from our beta customers show significant improvements in time-to-quota for new SDR and AE hires. We're currently working with several similar-sized SaaS organizations, and we've seen remarkable results.
>
> I'd love to schedule a 30-minute call with you to walk through our approach in more detail and discuss how we could potentially work together. I'm sure you have a lot on your plate, but I think this conversation would be valuable for both of us. Please let me know if you have some availability next week.
>
> Looking forward to connecting,
> Alex

**Fixed example (118 words):**

> Sarah,
>
> Your LinkedIn post last week on new-hire ramp time being misdiagnosed as a "content problem" is exactly the pattern I'm building around with early SaaS enablement teams.
>
> I noticed NorthStar hired 8 SDRs in the last 60 days — that's the kind of ramp that either goes well or it doesn't, and it usually comes down to how you measure it. Most enablement programs measure completion; the ones that work measure time-to-quota.
>
> I'm building tooling calibrated to the second. 15 minutes next Tuesday or Thursday to compare notes on what's worked for you?
>
> —Alex

### 2. Generic Opener

**Why it fails:** "I hope this email finds you well" and its variants are the universal tell of template email. The prospect has seen this opening 10,000 times. It signals the rest of the email is not worth reading.

**Bad examples:**
- "I hope this email finds you well."
- "I hope you're having a great week."
- "I came across your profile and was impressed by your work."
- "I wanted to reach out regarding..."
- "I've been following your company for some time now."

**Fixed example:**

Just open with the personalization angle directly. No preamble:

> Sarah — your LinkedIn post last week on ramp time caught my attention.

The greeting is the first name. The next sentence is the personalization. Skip the weather report.

---

### 3. Permission-Seeking Opener

**Why it fails:** "Would it be okay if I asked you a quick question?" makes the founder sound small. It also delays the actual content, so the prospect has to read past the permission request to find out what's happening.

**Bad examples:**
- "Would it be okay if I asked you a quick question?"
- "Would you mind if I reached out about..."
- "I hope you don't mind me connecting..."
- "Can I ask you something about..."

**Fixed example:**

Just ask the question or make the point. The prospect's time is respected by getting to the point, not by asking for permission to take it.

---

### 4. Feature Listing Instead of Pain Naming

**Why it fails:** Cold outreach that lists product features puts the focus on the founder's product, not the prospect's problem. The prospect does not care about features they have not agreed they have a problem for yet.

**Bad example:**

> Rampify offers:
> - Automated onboarding workflows
> - Real-time progress tracking
> - Custom learning paths
> - Integration with 50+ tools
> - Advanced analytics and reporting
> - White-glove onboarding support
>
> Would you like a demo?

**Fixed example:**

> The pattern I keep seeing: enablement teams measure onboarding completion, not time-to-quota. They feel good about a new SDR "finishing the training" in week 2, but the actual first-deal-closed date moves to month 4. That's the gap I'm building around. Would a 15-minute conversation on how you're measuring at NorthStar be useful?

Name the problem the prospect has. Do not list features. The features come up in the demo, not in the cold email.

---

### 5. Multi-CTA Ending

**Why it fails:** When the founder offers 3 possible next steps ("a demo, a white paper, a trial, a call, or an email with more info"), the prospect has to think about which one to pick. Cognitive load kills responses. Give one ask.

**Bad example:**

> Let me know if you'd like to jump on a quick call, or I'd be happy to send you a case study, or we have a free trial you can sign up for, or feel free to check out our website for more info. Looking forward to hearing from you!

**Fixed example:**

> 15 minutes next Tuesday or Thursday to compare notes? I'll keep it to the specific question and not pitch you.

One ask. One choice. Specific times. Low friction.

---

### 6. Sign-off Longer Than the Body

**Why it fails:** A 5-line corporate email signature with social links, phone numbers, physical address, and a "This email is confidential" disclaimer screams "marketing blast." It also shifts weight to the signature rather than the message.

**Bad example:**

> Thanks,
>
> Alex Rodriguez
> Co-Founder & CEO at Rampify
> Phone: (555) 123-4567 | Email: alex@rampify.com
> LinkedIn: linkedin.com/in/alexrodriguez | Twitter: @alexrodriguez
> Website: www.rampify.com
> 123 Main Street, San Francisco, CA 94105
>
> This email and any attachments are confidential...

**Fixed example:**

> —Alex
> Co-founder, Rampify

That's it. The prospect can look up the rest if they're interested. A minimalist sign-off signals confidence.

### 7. Fake Urgency

**Why it fails:** "Only available this week!" / "Limited spots remaining!" / "Offer expires Friday!" are manipulation tactics. The prospect sees through them instantly and trust degrades.

**Bad examples:**
- "We're only taking on 5 design partners this quarter!"
- "This offer expires at the end of the week."
- "Don't miss out — act fast!"
- "Last chance to lock in early pricing."

**Fixed approach:**

If the urgency is real (e.g., the founder genuinely has a cap on design partners), state it as a fact without the sales language:

> I'm taking on 5 design partners this quarter. If this sounds interesting, let's talk this week — otherwise I'd rather not leave it on the table and come back later.

If the urgency is manufactured, remove it entirely. There is no expiring offer in a real conversation.

---

### 8. Name-Dropping Irrelevant Customers

**Why it fails:** "We work with Acme Corp and BigCo" only earns trust if the prospect finds those names relevant. Name-dropping unrelated brands is credentialing theater.

**Bad example:**

> We've helped Acme Corp, BigCo, FamousCompany, and TechGiant improve their enablement processes.

**Fixed approach:**

If a relevant customer exists and is willing to be named, reference them specifically and with context:

> [Similar-sized SaaS company] ran into the same onboarding measurement gap at their 250-person mark. I'd be happy to share what they did if it's relevant.

If there is no relevant customer to reference (the founder has no case studies yet — which is common at this stage), say so honestly:

> I'm pre-launch and looking for design partners who care about this problem. No customers yet — which is exactly why I'm reaching out to you specifically.

Honesty about stage outperforms fake credentialing.

---

### 9. "Just Following Up" as a Follow-Up Subject

**Why it fails:** "Just following up" signals zero new value. The prospect sees the subject and knows there is nothing new to read. Auto-deletion territory.

**Bad examples (subject lines):**
- "Just following up"
- "Checking in"
- "Touching base"
- "Re: my previous email"
- "Following up on my note"

**Fixed approach:**

Every follow-up should have a subject line that names the new angle:

- "One more thing about your SDR hires" (new angle referenced)
- "Thought you'd find this useful" (new value promised)
- "A different way to think about ramp time" (new framing offered)
- "Last note on the onboarding question" (transparent breakup)

---

### 10. Claiming Research the Founder Didn't Actually Do

**Why it fails:** "I've been following your work closely" is usually a lie. If the prospect asks a follow-up question ("oh, which piece did you like?"), the founder can't answer and the relationship tanks. Even if the prospect never follows up, claiming research that didn't happen is a credibility risk.

**Bad examples:**
- "I've been following your work closely."
- "I've been a big fan of [Company]'s approach for a long time."
- "I've read all your posts on the subject."

**Fixed approach:**

Only claim research the founder actually did. Reference the specific thing:

> Your LinkedIn post last week on ramp-time-as-content-problem caught my attention.

Not:

> I've been following your work for a while.

If the founder has only done 5 minutes of LinkedIn lookup, the email should reflect 5 minutes of LinkedIn lookup, not a year of relationship.

---

### 11. Self-Congratulatory Intro

**Why it fails:** "We're the leading platform for X" / "We've helped 500+ companies Y" puts the focus on the founder's company, not the prospect. Cold emails that lead with self-congratulation are automatically scrolled past.

**Bad examples:**
- "Rampify is the leading platform for sales enablement analytics."
- "We've helped 500+ companies improve their onboarding processes."
- "Our innovative solution is transforming the industry."
- "We are a fast-growing startup backed by [VCs]."

**Fixed approach:**

Lead with the prospect. Mention the company only at the sign-off or in passing in the middle of the email. The entire opening should be about the prospect's world, not the founder's.

> Your LinkedIn post last week on ramp time... [continues with specific angle]

The founder's company is a footnote. The prospect is the point.

---

### 12. Vague Value Proposition

**Why it fails:** "We help companies like yours improve sales performance" could be said about any sales tool. The prospect has no way to know if this applies to them specifically. Generic value propositions earn zero attention.

**Bad examples:**
- "We help companies like yours improve sales performance."
- "We enable sales teams to work more efficiently."
- "We're transforming how companies think about enablement."
- "Our platform drives better outcomes for sales organizations."

**Fixed approach:**

Replace the vague value prop with one specific claim:

> Most onboarding programs measure completion instead of time-to-quota. I'm building tooling calibrated to the second — which is why I reached out specifically to someone running 8 new SDRs through their first 90 days.

One claim. One distinction. Grounded in a specific pattern. Tied to the prospect's situation.

---

## How To Use This List

Before showing any draft to the founder as a preview, scan the draft for each of the 12 anti-patterns. If any are present, rewrite the offending section before showing. Do not let the founder see a preview with any of these patterns in it — the founder will sometimes approve a draft with a known anti-pattern because they are tired, and a bad draft sent to a Tier A prospect is a worse outcome than an extra iteration.

The internal rule: **show only drafts that are anti-pattern-free.** Iteration happens before the preview, not after it.
