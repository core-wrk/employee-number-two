# Discovery Question Bank

This is an internal reference for the `pre-call-brief-builder` skill. It is a categorized library of validation questions for early founder-led calls, with guidance on when each works best. **Do not show this document to the founder.** Use it to draw questions from when building the brief, customizing each to the specific prospect's context.

---

## The Five Categories

Discovery questions fall into five categories. A good brief draws from at least 3 of them; a great brief draws from all 5:

1. **Pain Validation** — Does the prospect actually experience the pain the founder believes they do?
2. **Workaround Discovery** — What are they doing about it today?
3. **Willingness-to-Pay Signal** — Would they pay to solve it?
4. **Buying Authority** — Who decides?
5. **Trigger Events** — Why is it urgent now (or not)?

Each category answers a different question about whether the prospect is a real opportunity. Over-indexing on one category (usually pain validation) misses the others.

---

## Category 1: Pain Validation

**Purpose:** Hear the prospect describe the pain in their own words. The founder's goal is to learn whether the pain the founder is building around actually matches the pain the prospect experiences — and how they describe it (which feeds back into ICP refinement and brand voice work).

**The critical rule:** Do not ask leading questions. "Are you struggling with [specific pain]?" forces the prospect to either agree (anchoring) or push back (awkward). Open-ended questions get real answers.

### Question patterns

- **"Tell me about the last time [thing] came up for you."** — Asks for a specific story, which forces concrete details. Works best when "thing" is broad enough that the prospect can pick their own example.
- **"What's the thing that frustrates you most about [category]?"** — Lets the prospect prioritize for you. Their answer tells you what they actually care about.
- **"When you think about [category], what's the first thing that comes to mind?"** — Top-of-mind test. Surfaces whether the pain is actually salient.
- **"Walk me through what your day looks like when you're dealing with [category]."** — Asks for process. The process reveals the pain even when the prospect hasn't named it yet.
- **"What would you change about [current setup] if you could?"** — Prospective framing, less defensive than "what's wrong with it?"
- **"How often does [pain] actually come up?"** — Frequency check. Some pains are real but rare — those are not priority buying decisions.
- **"On a scale of 1–10, how painful is [specific pain] compared to other things on your plate?"** — Forces prioritization. Pain at a 3 while other things are at an 8 means the prospect is not a buyer for this solution.

### Worked examples (for the example ICP — VP Sales Enablement)

- "Walk me through the last time you had a new SDR start — what does their first 90 days actually look like today?"
- "When you think about your current enablement setup, what's the thing that frustrates you most?"
- "If you had to rank the top three problems you're trying to solve right now, where does onboarding new reps fall?"
- "Tell me about the last new hire who didn't work out — what did you learn from it?"

### Failure modes

- **Leading questions:** "You must be frustrated with [specific pain], right?" → the prospect will either agree politely (false positive) or feel trapped (bad rapport)
- **Too abstract:** "How do you think about enablement?" → vague, prospect gives a vague answer
- **Multi-part:** "How do you onboard new hires, what tools do you use, and how do you measure success?" → prospect picks one part and forgets the rest

## Category 2: Workaround Discovery

**Purpose:** Find out what the prospect is doing about the pain today. Their current workaround tells you the competitive landscape, the switching cost, and the threshold of friction they are willing to tolerate before they switch.

**The critical insight:** Every prospect has a workaround. Even "we do nothing about it" is a workaround. The workaround is the competitor. If the prospect is currently using [Competitor X], the founder is competing with X. If the prospect is currently using "a Google Doc my SDR manager updates weekly," the founder is competing with the Google Doc. The Google Doc is often harder to displace than you'd think.

### Question patterns

- **"How are you handling this today?"** — Direct but effective.
- **"What tools are you using right now for [category]?"** — Surfaces the tool stack.
- **"Walk me through your current process for [specific thing]."** — Process-based, reveals hidden steps.
- **"What did you use before [current tool]?"** — Surfaces churn patterns (why did they switch once, and could they switch again?)
- **"If you had to describe your current setup to someone joining your team, how would you explain it?"** — Forces clarity; often reveals the workaround is duct-taped together.
- **"What's the thing you do manually that you wish was automated?"** — Surfaces friction points.
- **"How long have you been doing it this way?"** — Reveals whether the workaround is entrenched or recently set up (recent setups are easier to displace).

### Worked examples

- "How are you measuring whether your onboarding program is working right now?"
- "When a new SDR starts on Monday, walk me through what happens in their first week at NorthStar."
- "What tools are you using for onboarding today? And are those the ones you actively chose, or what you inherited?"
- "If the onboarding tool you're currently using disappeared tomorrow, what would you actually miss about it?" (reveals the tool's real value, not the marketing value)

### Failure modes

- **Jumping to comparison:** "How would [our product] be better than [their tool]?" → presumptuous at this stage; you don't know enough yet
- **Judging the workaround:** "That sounds really inefficient" → makes the prospect defensive
- **Ignoring the workaround:** skipping straight to your product without understanding what they're replacing

## Category 3: Willingness-to-Pay Signal

**Purpose:** Learn whether the prospect has budget, would allocate budget, or has any economic interest in solving the pain. This is **not** asking "would you buy it?" (the prospect will almost always say yes out of politeness). It's surfacing economic signal indirectly.

**The critical insight:** Willingness-to-pay is revealed through small questions, not big ones. Asking "would you pay $500/month?" is bad because the prospect has no context. Asking "when was the last time you got budget approved for an enablement tool, and what did it take?" is good because it surfaces the actual buying motion without trying to close.

### Question patterns

- **"If this were solved tomorrow, what would change about how you work?"** — Value-of-solution framing. Good answers name specific business outcomes (time saved, deals closed, retention improved). Weak answers are vague ("it would be easier").
- **"When was the last time you got budget approved for a tool in this space? What did that process look like?"** — Reveals the actual buying motion and budget ceiling.
- **"What does your current stack cost you — not in dollars, but in time and hassle?"** — Reframes cost from just-dollars to total-cost-of-current-state, which is where your product's value has to come from.
- **"If you could spend $10k/year on something that saved you [specific pain], what would you want that thing to do?"** — Hypothetical budget, reveals priorities.
- **"What's the highest-value hour you spend each week on [category]?"** — Opportunity cost framing.
- **"Walk me through the ROI math you'd need to see to justify buying a new tool in this space."** — Direct but requires the prospect to be senior enough to articulate it.

### Worked examples

- "If you could snap your fingers and have one thing change about how new reps ramp at NorthStar, what would it be?"
- "When you bought [current tool] originally, what was the ROI math you presented?"
- "What does NorthStar spend on enablement tools right now — not to the dollar, but in rough shape?"
- "If you had a 30% faster ramp time on your new SDR cohort, what would that be worth to NorthStar in quota dollars?"

### Failure modes

- **Direct price asks:** "Would you pay $X?" → prospect has no context, answer is meaningless
- **Presumptuous value claims:** "We save companies $500k a year on enablement" → the prospect doesn't believe it yet
- **Skipping this category:** "We'll talk about pricing later" → you need the signal now to know if the prospect is a buyer at all

## Category 4: Buying Authority

**Purpose:** Find out who decides. In B2B, the person who signs the contract, the person who uses the product, the person who champions it internally, and the person who blocks it are often four different people. You need to know which one your prospect is.

**The critical insight:** Asking "are you the decision-maker?" is useless — everyone says yes because no one wants to admit they can't make decisions. Asking "walk me through how a tool like this would get bought" surfaces the actual process.

### Question patterns

- **"Walk me through how a tool like this would get bought at [company]."** — Process-based, reveals the real chain.
- **"The last time you brought a new tool into [company], who all had to weigh in?"** — Historical pattern, factual not aspirational.
- **"Who else would this need to go through besides you?"** — Direct but softened by "besides you" (which assumes the prospect is involved).
- **"If you wanted to bring this in tomorrow, what would stop it from happening?"** — Reveals the blocker, not the enabler.
- **"Does [tool category] live under Sales, Marketing, RevOps, or somewhere else at [company]?"** — Org-chart question, reveals the buying center.
- **"When you brought in [current tool], how long did that process take and who was involved?"** — Anchors in a real past decision.

### Worked examples

- "When you evaluate a new tool in this space, walk me through who's typically involved in that decision at NorthStar."
- "If you wanted to bring an enablement tool in tomorrow, what would the path look like — who would you need to bring along?"
- "How did NorthStar end up on [Competitor A]? Who drove that decision?"
- "Does your VP Sales typically weigh in on enablement tool decisions, or is it fully in your lane?"

### Failure modes

- **"Are you the decision-maker?"** → useless answer
- **Assuming the prospect is the buyer:** proceeding with a pitch without understanding the buying chain
- **Skipping this category because it feels awkward:** this is the category founders skip most and regret most

## Category 5: Trigger Events

**Purpose:** Find out what happened in the last 30–90 days that made this worth a conversation. If the prospect responded to a cold outreach, something triggered the response — learn what.

**The critical insight:** Prospects who are responding to cold outreach are often doing so because of a recent event the founder doesn't know about. Surfacing the trigger event gives the founder context for everything else in the call — and often reveals whether the window of urgency is weeks or months.

### Question patterns

- **"What had to happen for this to become a priority for you?"** — Direct trigger question.
- **"You mentioned this is 'a real problem right now' — what made it urgent this month?"** — Builds on a phrase the prospect already used (from cold outreach reply).
- **"When you saw my email, what specifically caught your attention?"** — Surfaces what about the outreach resonated.
- **"What's changed in the last 3 months that makes you thinking about this differently?"** — Temporal framing.
- **"Before the recent [company event — funding, hire, launch], was this top of mind, or is it new?"** — Before-and-after.
- **"If we'd talked 6 months ago, would this conversation have been different?"** — Counterfactual; reveals the delta.

### Worked examples

- "You mentioned this is 'a real problem right now' — what made it urgent this month?"
- "When you saw my note about time-to-quota measurement, what made you reply? I'd love to know what specifically landed."
- "Did the recent SDR hires push this up the priority list, or was it already there?"
- "If we'd had this conversation 3 months ago, before the new SDR class started, would it have gone differently?"

### Failure modes

- **Not asking:** missing the trigger is missing the most important context for the whole call
- **Assuming the trigger:** "you must be panicking about the new SDRs" → projection, and often wrong
- **Asking but moving on:** the trigger answer should shape everything that follows in the call

## How To Combine Categories By Call Length

### 15-minute intro call (3 questions total)

Pick ONE from each of: Pain Validation, Workaround Discovery, Trigger Events.

Example combination:
1. "Walk me through the last time [pain] came up for you." (Pain Validation)
2. "How are you handling this today?" (Workaround Discovery)
3. "What made you want to have this conversation now?" (Trigger Events)

Skip Willingness-to-Pay and Buying Authority for the intro — too much to fit, and the prospect doesn't trust you enough yet.

### 30-minute call (5–7 questions)

Pick 2 from Pain Validation, 1–2 from Workaround, 1 from Willingness-to-Pay, 1 from Buying Authority, 1 from Trigger Events.

### 60-minute working session (8–12 questions)

Pick 3 from Pain Validation, 2 from Workaround, 2 from Willingness-to-Pay, 2 from Buying Authority, 1–2 from Trigger Events.

## Universal Rules For Every Question

1. **Open-ended, not yes/no.** "Would you pay for this?" is a yes/no — useless. "If you were going to pay for a tool in this space, what would it have to do to be worth it?" is open-ended — valuable.
2. **Specific, not abstract.** "How do you think about enablement?" is abstract. "Walk me through the last new hire who didn't work out" is specific.
3. **Their language, not yours.** If the prospect calls it "ramp time," don't call it "onboarding velocity."
4. **One question at a time, on the actual call.** Combining questions (asking a 3-part question) forces the prospect to pick one and lose the other two.
5. **Follow-ups are free.** The best questions are ones you didn't plan — the follow-up to their answer. The planned list is a starting point, not a script.
