# Design Partner Framework Reference

This is an internal reference for the `design-partner-advisor` skill. It is the canonical definition of what a design partnership is, what it is not, how it differs from beta/customer relationships, the asymmetric exchange, when to offer one, and when to stop. With worked examples from well-known startups. **Do not show this document to the founder.** Use it to ground the conversation in pattern-matching from how the best early-stage founders have structured these relationships.

---

## What A Design Partnership Is

A design partnership is a **co-development relationship** between a founder and an early customer where the customer gives more than a typical customer would (time, candor, willingness to be wrong) in exchange for more than a typical customer would receive (influence, access, pricing concession, maybe small equity).

The key word is **co-development**. A design partner is not a test user. They are not a beta tester. They are not a reference customer. They are a partner in building the product itself — the product is still forming, and the partner's specific reality shapes what it becomes.

### The three things that make it a design partnership (not something else)

1. **The product is still forming.** If the product is done and the founder is selling it, the partner is a customer, not a design partner. Design partnerships require that the product would genuinely be different without this specific partner's influence.
2. **The exchange is asymmetric in both directions.** A design partner gives more (time, access, candor) and gets more (influence, pricing, founder attention). A relationship where only the founder gives is "free pilot" (bad). A relationship where only the partner gives is "reference customer" (different thing).
3. **The partner has real influence over direction.** "We'll consider your feedback" is not influence. A design partner should have specific mechanisms — a standing call, roadmap review rights, right of refusal on a category of decisions, direct build access.

## What A Design Partnership Is NOT

### Not a beta user

A beta user tests an existing product and provides feedback. The product is not being shaped by them — it's being validated by them. Beta users don't need founder attention, pricing concessions, or influence. They need a working product and a feedback channel.

### Not an early customer

An early customer pays for an existing product. The founder is not trying to learn from them — the founder is trying to serve them. Early customers expect reliability, support, and a working product. They do not expect to influence the roadmap heavily.

### Not a free pilot

A free pilot is "try the product without paying." That is not a design partnership — it's discounting masked as partnership language. If the partner is not giving something meaningful in exchange (time, candor, access, logo rights), it's a giveaway, not a partnership.

### Not a reference customer

A reference customer has used the product and agrees to be named or quoted. That is a marketing asset, not a design partnership. Reference customers typically come AFTER a design partnership ends, not during it.

## The Asymmetric Exchange

### What the founder gives

**Founder attention (the most valuable thing)** — a specific amount of founder time, typically named. "2 hours a month of direct founder access" is specific. "We'll be in touch occasionally" is not. At early stage, founder attention is the single most valuable thing a partner gets. Do not hide it behind vague language.

**Influence over product direction** — specific mechanisms. Common patterns:
- Weekly or monthly roadmap review calls
- Right of refusal on a specific category of decisions (e.g., "you can veto changes to the onboarding flow")
- Direct access to the product build queue — seeing features before they ship, naming priorities
- Co-authorship of a specific feature

**Pricing concession** — usually meaningful. Common patterns:
- Free for the design period (6–12 months)
- 50%+ discount during the partnership, with lock-in price when it ends
- Free forever with usage caps
- Grandfathered pricing at the design-period rate permanently

**Early access** — ahead of the general rollout. Usually meaningful only if the partnership is pre-launch or pre-GA.

**Logo rights** — the partner can say they are working with the founder, use the founder's brand in their own public communications, etc. Usually symmetric with the founder's ability to use the partner's logo.

**Small equity or warrant (rare)** — in a small number of strategic cases, the founder gives a small equity grant or warrant to a strategic design partner. This is rare and should only be considered when the partner is bringing strategic value beyond the partnership itself (a well-known brand, a critical market, a category-defining relationship). Most founders should NOT offer equity.

### What the partner gives

**Time commitment (specific)** — "2 hours a month" is specific. "They'll be engaged" is not. Typical good commitments:
- 2–4 hours per month of direct conversation
- Willingness to test new features and give feedback within 1 week of release
- A standing monthly or biweekly call

**Candor** — this is harder than it sounds. Polite partners are worse than honest ones. The founder needs someone who will say "this is broken" or "this is wrong" without softening it. The agreement should explicitly name the expectation of honesty.

**Access and information** — the partner gives the founder access to their real workflows, real data (or anonymized equivalents), real context. A design partner who treats the engagement as a meeting series misses the point.

**Logo and case study rights** — the founder can publicly name the partner as a design partner (usually after a 30–60 day embargo from the start, so the partner can time their own public announcement). Often includes the right to publish a case study after the engagement.

**Right to share learnings publicly** — the founder can discuss what they learned from the engagement publicly (usually anonymized, sometimes named with permission).

## Worked Examples From Well-Known Startups

### Stripe's early API design partners

Stripe in 2010 built the API with direct input from a small number of early customers — developers building products that needed payments and were willing to provide deep feedback on the API design. These partners shaped specific API decisions (webhook patterns, error handling, retry logic) and in exchange got founder access, pricing concessions, and influence over what Stripe became. The partners did not call it a "design partnership" — it was a founder-led conversation with early users — but the structure was exactly this: asymmetric exchange, co-development, product shaped by specific partner realities.

**Lesson:** Design partnerships often don't use the formal language. What matters is the structure: specific partners, specific influence, specific exchange.

### Notion's early enterprise design partners

Notion's early enterprise design partners got founder access and the ability to shape enterprise-specific features (SSO, permissions, admin tools) that Notion would not have built without specific partner input. The partners got founder access, pricing, and influence. Notion got the enterprise feature set shaped by real customer realities rather than by founder guesses.

**Lesson:** Design partnerships can be vertical-specific. A founder doesn't need to design-partner with everyone — they can pick one segment where they need deep input and structure partnerships there.

### Figma's early designer design partners

Figma's early beta was effectively a design partnership — designers at specific companies got early access to a product that was barely functional, in exchange for their time and candor. Figma used their feedback to shape what the product became. The "payment" to the designers was access to a tool that might change their workflow — not a dollar concession, but a real one.

**Lesson:** Sometimes the concession is access itself, not pricing. If the product is genuinely novel, early access can be the entire offer.

## When To Offer A Design Partnership

### Good conditions

- **You're still validating the core value proposition.** If the founder is not sure whether the product solves a real problem, design partners are the validation mechanism.
- **You have specific questions that need specific answers.** "Does this feature make sense?" is not specific. "Does this workflow compression from 8 steps to 3 actually save time, or does it feel like losing control?" is specific.
- **The partner's specific reality can shape the product.** A partner in the exact ICP with willingness to be honest can shape the product in ways that customer feedback from aggregate users cannot.
- **You can afford to give away founder time and pricing concessions.** If the founder has 20 hours a week of total capacity and design partnerships will eat 8 of them, that's a problem. If it eats 2, it's fine.

### Bad conditions

- **You're past product-market fit.** If the product is working and customers are paying, design partnerships become a tax. Skip them.
- **You need revenue more than you need learning.** A founder at burn-critical runway should not be giving away free product to design partners — they should be selling.
- **The prospect is asking for free product, not partnership.** If the prospect's interest is "I want this for free," that's a disqualifier, not a design partnership opportunity.
- **The founder can't commit to the founder-attention part.** If the founder can't give 2 hours a month per partner, the partnership will fail — partners feel ghosted and leave.

---

## When To Stop Offering Design Partnerships

The founder should stop offering NEW design partnerships when:

- The sales motion is repeatable — the founder knows how to close customers without heavy personal involvement
- Customers are willing to pay full price without asking for concessions
- Aggregate feedback is more useful than individual partner input
- The founder's hours become too valuable to give away at design-partner rates

Existing design partnerships can continue (and should, honoring the original terms) but new ones should not be offered. This transition point is usually 12–24 months after the first partnership, depending on how quickly the product finds its footing.

## Common Failure Modes

1. **Over-offering** — founder promises so much (weekly calls, unlimited access, free forever, equity, influence) that any design partnership destroys the founder's capacity to build. **Fix:** scope tightly upfront.
2. **Under-offering** — founder offers so little (10% off, "we'll consider your feedback") that no prospect wants to participate. **Fix:** push the founder to commit to specific, meaningful concessions.
3. **Confused framing** — founder calls everything a design partnership, including normal customers and free pilots. **Fix:** force the distinction between partner / beta / customer in every conversation.
4. **No exit plan** — the partnership continues indefinitely, the partner continues to get free product, the founder forgets when it was supposed to end. **Fix:** time-bounded terms and clear success/exit criteria.
5. **Legal over-engineering** — founder tries to make the conceptual agreement bulletproof with 20 pages of contract language. **Fix:** 1–2 pages max for the conceptual skeleton; legal drafting is a separate step.
6. **Asymmetric ask, symmetric give** — founder expects design-partner-level commitment from the partner but offers customer-level terms in return. **Fix:** honor the asymmetry in both directions.
